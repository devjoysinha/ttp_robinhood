10. Work Problems

10.8.1 Determining a Combined Rate of a Group of Objects When Given Three or more Combined Rates

In the previous two combined rates lessons, we found combined rates by substituting values into the formula RateObject 1+RateObject 2+RateObject 3+. . .+RateObject n=Combined RateAll n objects.

However, as combined rate problems become more challenging, we will be given information regarding several combined rates, and we must determine the combined rate of all the objects.

In this problem type, substitution will be too tedious, so the optimal method will be as follows:

1. Add all the combined rate equations provided.

2. Divide by a common factor to determine the combined rate of all the objects.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

When solving questions where we are given three separate combined rates and asked to determine the combined rate for the entire group, we do the following:

1. Add all the combined rate equations provided.

2. Divide by a common factor to determine the combined rate of all the objects.

Lets practice this concept with the following mini-example:

Working together at their constant respective rates, workers X and Y can paint a house in 6 hours. Working together, workers Y and Z can paint the same house in 9 hours, and workers X and Z can paint it in 10 hours. How long would it take workers X, Y and Z, working together, to paint the house?

Let the individual rates of workers X, Y and Z be 1X, 1Y, and 1Z, respectively.

To find the time it takes the 3 workers to complete the job together, we need their combined rate.

In other words, we need the value of 1X+1Y+1Z.

If workers X and Y can paint the house in 6 hours, their combined rate is worktime=1house6 hours=16househour

Thus, we have equation 1: 1X+1Y=16 (we can add the units later).

If workers Y and Z can paint the house in 9 hours, their combined rate is worktime=1house9 hours=19househour

Thus, we have equation 2: 1Y+1Z=19 (we can add the units later).

If workers X and Z can paint the house in 10 hours, their combined rate is worktime=1house10 hours=110househour

Thus, we have equation 3: 1X+1Z=110 (we can add the units later).

Alone, each of the three equations, 1X+1Y=16, 1Y+1Z=19 and 1X+1Z=110, does not allow us to determine the combined rate, 1X+1Y+1Z

However, notice what happens when we add the three equations:

(1X+1Y)+(1Y+1Z)+(1X+1Z)=16+19+1102X+2Y+2Z=1590+1090+9902X+2Y+2Z=3490

At this point, we can factor 2 out of the left side of the equation and then divide both sides by 2:

2X+2Y+2Z=34902(1X+1Y+1Z)=34901X+1Y+1Z=349021X+1Y+1Z=349012=34180=1790

The combined rate of workers X, Y, and Z is 1X+1Y+1Z=1790househour.

Thus, the time for all 3 workers to paint the house is workrate=11790=19017=9017=5517hours.

Text Solution:

Question Stem Analysis:

If Machine E can complete an order in e hours, then its rate is worktime=1ordere hours=1eorderhour.

If Machine F can complete an order in f hours, then its rate is worktime=1orderf hours=1forderhour.

If Machine G can complete an order in g hours, then its rate is worktime=1orderg hours=1gorderhour.

To find the time for the 3 machines, working together, to order the job, we need their combined rate. In other words, we need the value of 1e+1f+1g.

Since we are told machines F and G can complete the order in 5 hours, their combined rate is 15orderhour.

We can use this to write:

Equation 1: 1e+1f=15 (we can add the units later).

Statement One Alone:

Working together, machines F and G can complete the order in 6 hours.

This tells us their combined rate = worktime=1order6 hours=16orderhour

We can use this to write:

Equation 2: 1f+1g=16 (we can add the units later).

From equations 1 and 2, we know that 1e+1f=15 and 1f+1g=16.

These two equations dont provide enough information to find the value of 1e+1f+1g.

Statement one alone is not sufficient. Eliminate answer choices A and D.

Statement Two Alone:

Working together, machines E and G can complete the order in 10 hours.

This tells us their combined rate = worktime=1order10 hours=110orderhour

We can use this to write:

Equation 3: 1e+1g=110.

From equations 1 and 3, we know that 1e+1f=15 and 1e+1g=110.

These two equations dont provide enough information to find the value of 1e+1f+1g.

Statement two alone is not sufficient. Eliminate answer choice B.

Statements One and Two Together:

Combining the two statements, we get three equations: 1e+1f=15, 1f+1g=16, and 1e+1g=110.

When we add the three equations, we get:

(1e+1f)+(1f+1g)+(1e+1g)=15+16+1102e+2f+2g=630+530+3302e+2f+2g=1430


At this point, we can factor 2 out of the left side of the equation and then divide both sides by 2.

2e+2f+2g=14302(1e+1f+1g)=14301e+1f+1g=143021e+1f+1g=1430121e+1f+1g=1460=730

The combined rate of machines E, F, and G is 1e+1f+1g=1460=730orderhour.

Thus, the time for all 3 machines to complete the order is workrate=1730=1307=307=427hours.

The two statements combined are sufficient to answer the question.

Correct answer: C

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
