10. Work Problems

10.7.4 Percent of a job done and fraction of a job done

In a work problem, you could be asked to determine the percent or fraction of a certain job that one of the workers performed. For example, what if you and a friend built a bookcase? For that job, you worked at a constant rate of 14bookcasehour, and your friend worked at a constant rate of 18bookcasehour. If you both worked for the same amount of time, what fraction of the bookcase did you build?

To answer such a question, we need to first determine the total work that is performed. Since you both work for the same time, lets let the work time be t. Thus, since Work = Rate Time or w = r t., you built 14t of the bookcase, and your friend built 18t of the bookcase. Therefore, the total work done is 14t+18t=38t. We can now easily determine the fraction of the total job you did.

WorkYouWorkTotal=t4bookcase3t8bookcase=(tbookcase483tbookcase)=812=23

We can see you performed 23 of the job. Now, if the problem asked for the percent of the job that you did, we could easily convert 23 to a percent by multiplying it by 100:

Percent of the job you did = 2310067%

Thus, you did about 67% of the job. Its important to see that in this problem it is not necessary to determine the value of t before determining the percent or fraction of the job that you completed. This is important enough to warrant a little more discussion.

The reason we do not need to determine t comes from the fact that Work = Rate Time or w = r t. Thus for you, w = 14t, and for your friend w = 18t. That is, 14t and 18t are work values. Therefore, the total work done is 14t+18t=38t. Since 38t is also a work value, t43t8100 will allow us to calculate the percent of the work you did without ever needing to determine the value of t.

Since all of the work values are in terms of t, we can calculate a percentage. The key to this problem is to realize that one need not determine t to find the percentage each machine contributed. As we are solving, in particular, for the percent of the total job that was completed by Machine A, lets first add together all the work in terms of t to get the total:

WorkTotal=WorkMachineA+WorkMachineB+WorkMachineCWorkTotal=t8engine+t4engine+t4engineWorkTotal=5t8engine

Then we can determine the percentage done by Machine A.

WorkMachineAWorkTotal=t8engine5t8engine=(tengine885tengine)=15=20%

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
