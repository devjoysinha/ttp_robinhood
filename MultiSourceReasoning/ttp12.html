6. Multi Source Reasoning

6.12 Be Careful in Processing Details in Tables and Graphs

There are some basic strategies that we can use to help us do two key things when dealing with tables, charts, and graphs:

1. efficiently locate the information we need AND

2. ensure that we arent missing key details.

First, when we initially read through a prompt that contains a table, it is helpful to notice whether the table is sorted in a particular way. For example:

- in ascending order by the year column

- alphabetically by company name

- by project cost from greatest to least

and so on.

Remember, tables in MSR can contain lots of information. So, noticing how the information is arranged can help us to more quickly find the specific data points we need when we return to the table to answer a question.

Noticing whether a table is sorted in a particular way helps us more efficiently find information that we need to answer an MSR question.

Additionally, we should be aware that tables, charts, and graphs in MSR often contain similar-looking information right next to each other.

For example, a table may contain a column of dollar amounts that correspond to regularly priced items right next to a column of dollar amounts that correspond to sale-priced items.

Or, a table may contain a column of population numbers for the year 2021 right next to a column of population numbers for the year 2022.

A graph or chart may contain many different elements that are all quite similar-looking and packed together, making it confusing to understand.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

MSR tables, graphs, and charts often contain very similar-looking data next to each other and may be packed with details.

As a result, it can be surprisingly easy to pull values from the wrong part of a table, chart, or graph when answering an MSR question.

So, its good practice to double-check the labels on different parts of the graphic before we pull values from those areas to answer a question.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

Double-check the label on different parts of tables, charts, and graphs before pulling values from those areas to answer a question.

Additionally, key details that can make or break our ability to correctly answer a question are sometimes contained within the title of a table, chart, or graph in an MSR prompt.

For example, say a question requires us to determine a total cost per employee. So, we diligently locate and tally all the relevant values in a given table. However, we either forget or neglect to notice that the table is labeled as presenting information corresponding to every 10 employees.

By missing that small detail, per 10 employees, in the title of the table, we miss the simple yet crucial last step of determining the total cost per employee: dividing by 10.

We often may not think too much about the title of a graphic after we have initially glanced at it, instead focusing on the information contained within the graphic. However, we must be aware that in MSR, a key detail for answering a question can appear in a title.

Text Solution:

Tab 2 presents a graph with two y-axes for the different colored dots. The red dots tell us the number of family doctors in Urbanville from 2009 to 2019. The blue dots tell us the number of family doctors per 1,000 Urbanville residents from 2009 to 2019.

Once we know the number of family doctors in 2014 and the ratio of family doctors to 1,000 Urbanville residents in 2014, we can determine Urbanvilles population in 2014.

Lets start with the red dot representing information for 2014.

We see that the red dot corresponding to 2014 also corresponds to there being 42 family doctors. So, Urbanville had 42 family doctors in 2014.

Now lets locate with the blue dot that indicates the number of family doctors per 1,000 Urbanville residents in 2014.

The blue dot corresponding to 2014 also corresponds to there being 1.6 family doctors per 1,000 Urbanville residents.

So, in 2014, the ratio of the number of family doctors to the number of Urbanville residents was 1.6 to 1,000.

We also know there were 42 family doctors in 2014.

So, if we let x = Urbanvilles population in 2014, we can use equivalent ratios in the form (number of doctors):(number of citizens) to answer the question.

42:x = 1.6:1,000

To solve this equation for x, well express the ratios as fractions:

42x=1.61,0001.6x=42,000x=42,0001.6x=26,250

So, Urbanvilles population was 26,250 in 2014.

Correct answer: C

TWO STRATEGIES REVENUE AND EXPENSES
Showing:
Two Strategies Revenue and Expenses

Fizz Cola and Zoom Cola are the only sodas sold on the island nation of Thermania. The two sodas are available only in 355ml cans from vending machines owned by the two companies. Prior to 2010, both companies charged the same retail price for each can of cola. The two companies have no other revenue sources.

On December 31, 2009, the soda producers shared with their respective shareholders the same 10-year goals:

Goal #1: Sell more than 50% of the cans of soda sold on Thermania.

Goal #2: Generate annual revenue greater than the previous years revenue.

Although the two soda companies had the same 10-year goals, their strategies were significantly different.

Fizz Colas executives decided that, from 2010 to 2019 inclusive, the company would double its 2009 annual advertising budget. To offset this additional expense, the company will increase by 50% the retail price of each can of soda.

Conversely, Zoom Colas executives decided that, since the majority of Thermanias citizens live below the poverty level, the company would increase its sales by decreasing by 25% the retail price of its sodas. To compensate for a potential revenue decrease, the executives decided that, from 2010 to 2019 inclusive, the companys annual advertising budget would be 50% less than its 2009 advertising budget.

The above changes went into effect the following day, on January 1, 2010.

Each year, from 2009 to 2019 inclusive, both companies spent their entire advertising budget.

During the 10-year period from 2010 to 2019 inclusive, in how many years was goal #2 achieved by both Fizz Cola and Zoom Cola?

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
