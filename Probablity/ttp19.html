17. Probability

17.12 The probability of at least some number of events occurring

Earlier in the chapter, we learned about at least probability problems. As you may recall, the phrase at least means equal to or greater than.

To start, lets refresh our memory of these question types by reviewing the sample problem stems below:

From a classroom containing 4 boys and 4 girls, if 4 students are selected at random, what is the probability that at least 3 boys are selected?

We are seeking the probability that either 3 boys and 1 girl are selected, or 4 boys are selected. Lets look at another example.

At a birthday party, there are 3 bowls of vanilla ice cream and 7 bowls of chocolate ice cream on a table. If 3 bowls of ice cream are randomly selected from the table, what is the probability that at least 2 bowls of vanilla ice cream are selected?

We are seeking the probability that either 2 bowls of vanilla ice cream and 1 bowl of chocolate ice cream are selected, or 3 bowls of vanilla ice cream are selected.

If we had to solve the two problems above, we could use the method learned in 17.10: At Least Probability Problems. However, we also could use combinations to solve these problems. The good news is that when using combinations, we will rely on many of the skills already learned in the past few lessons. Lets now completely solve the following mini-example. When doing so, we will show how to determine the probability when using both the approach taught in the At Least Probability Problems chapter and the combinations approach.

In a florists vase, there are 3 roses and 4 tulips. If the florist randomly selects 3 flowers from the vase, what is the probability that at least 2 of those flowers will be roses?

At Least Probability Problems Approach

We need to determine the probability that when 3 flowers are selected, at least 2 of the flowers will be roses. In other words, we need to determine the probability that 2 roses and 1 tulip are selected OR 3 roses are selected. We can let R = a rose is selected and T = a tulip is selected. Lets now express the scenarios below and determine the probability of each.

Scenario 1:

The probability that 2 roses and 1 tulip are selected.

We need to determine the number of outcomes in which we obtain RRT, not necessarily in that order. RRT can be arranged in 3!2!=3 ways, so there are three possible outcomes in which two roses and one tulip are selected.

Next, we can determine the probability of one possible outcome:

One possible outcome is RRT, which has a probability of 372645=435.

To determine the actual probability of selecting 2 roses and 1 tulip, we calculate as follows:

number of possible outcomes producing the event probability of just one outcome

3435=1235

Now, lets consider scenario 2.

Scenario 2:

The probability that 3 roses are selected.

We need to determine the number of possible outcomes of RRR. Notice that this outcome can occur in only one way. Thus, the probability is the following:

372615=6210=135

Lastly, we can add together the probability of each scenario.

P(Scenario 1) + P(Scenario 2) = P( selecting at least 2 roses in 3 selections)

1235+135=1335

Lets now solve the problem using combinations.

Combination Method

We need to determine the following 2 scenarios:

Scenario 1:

P(2 roses and 1 tulip)=number of ways 2 roses and 1 tulip are selectedtotal number of ways 3 flowers are selected from 7

Scenario 2:

P(3 roses)=number of ways 3 roses are selectedtotal number of ways 3 flowers are selected from 7

Lets start by determining the number of favorable outcomes from scenario 1.

We need to determine the number of ways to select 2 roses and 1 tulip. We will use the box and fill method:

Scenario 1: Number of ways to select 2 roses out of 3:

C32=322!=322=3 ways

Number of ways to select 1 tulip out of 4:

C41=4 ways

The total number of ways to select the roses and tulips is the product of the number of ways that the two roses can be selected and the number of ways that the one tulip can be selected, which is 3 4 = 12 ways. Thus, the number of favorable outcomes is 12. Next, lets determine the total number of outcomes.

We can determine the number of ways to select three flowers from seven. We will use the box and fill method:

C73=7653!=76532=75=35 ways

Thus, the probability of scenario 1, is 1235. Lets determine scenario 2.

Scenario 2: Number of ways to select 3 roses out of 3:

C33=1 way

Next, we need to determine the total number of outcomes. Since we already have done this in the previous scenario, we know that the total number of outcomes is 35. Thus, the probability of scenario 2 is 135.

Lastly, we can add together the probability of each scenario.

P(Scenario 1) + P(Scenario 2) = P(selecting at least 2 roses in 3 selections)

1235+135=1335

As we can see, both methods are equally viable in solving these question types. In the examples that follow, we will solve each problem by using both of the methods.

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
