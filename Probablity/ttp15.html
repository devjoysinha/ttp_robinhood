17. Probability

17.11 Blending combinatorics and probability

In this lesson we will discuss how combinatorics can be used to solve certain probability questions. We’ll rely on a formula that we learned earlier in this chapter:

Probability of an event=number of favorable outcomestotal number of outcomes

In this section, we will use combinatorics to directly calculate probabilities; we’ll do this by first calculating the numerator of the fraction (favorable outcomes) and then by calculating the denominator of the fraction (total outcomes). Finally, we’ll perform the indicated division, and the result will be the desired probability. Let’s start out by practicing this method with a simple probability question.

There are 5 bottles of orange soda and 4 bottles of grape soda in a cooler. What is the probability that a randomly selected bottle will be grape?

We need to determine the following:

P(grape soda)=number of outcomes in which a grape soda could be chosentotal number of ways in which a soda could be chosen

Let’s use the combination formula to determine the numerator (favorable outcomes). There are 4 bottles of grape soda, and we want to choose one of them. Thus, there are4C1 = 4 ways in which a grape soda can be chosen.

Next, let’s calculate the denominator (total outcomes). There are 9 total sodas that we’re choosing from. Thus, there are9C1 = 9 ways in which any one soda could be chosen.

Now, let’s use the formula to calculate P(grape soda):

P(grape soda)=number of outcomes in which a grape soda could be chosentotal number of ways in which a soda could be chosenP(grape soda)=49

At this point you may be wondering why we used the combination method to solve the problem above. After all, you could have simply reasoned that there are 4 grape sodas and 9 total sodas, yielding a probability of 49. And it’s true that in simple probability problems the combination method isn’t useful. However, in the problems that follow you’ll see that the combination approach is efficient (and preferable), especially when tackling dependent probability questions with multiple trials.

Let’s now practice a more challenging problem containing multiple trials (i. e. we are making more than one selection). We will solve it using both the “traditional” method taught in the lesson “Accounting for Multiple Outcomes” as well as the combination method, so you will clearly see how efficient the combination method is when solving these problem types.

There are 4 girls and 5 boys in Mrs. Peabody’s middle school class. If Mrs. Peabody randomly picks 3 children from the class, what is the probability that she will pick exactly 1 girl?

“Traditional Method” Solution

We are given that there are 4 girls and 5 boys in a class. We need to determine the probability that when 3 students are selected, 1 girl is chosen. In other words, we need to determine the probability that 1 girl and 2 boys are chosen or the probability of girl, boy, boy (but not necessarily in that order). Letting G = a girl is selected and B = a boy is selected, we can determine the number of outcomes for selecting exactly one girl and two boys by determining the number of ways to arrange the letters GBB.

GBB can be arranged in 3!2!=3 ways, so there are three possible orders in which we could select one girl and two boys.

Next, we can determine the probability of one possible outcome:

One possible outcome is GBB. To determine the probability of that outcome, we can do the following:

Since there are 4 girls and 9 total students, the probability of selecting a girl first is 49. For the next selection, there are 8 students left and 5 total boys, so the probability of selecting a boy next is 58. Finally, since there are now 4 boys left and 7 total students left, the probability of selecting a boy last is 47. Thus:

P(GBB) = 49×58×47=29×51×17=1063

Finally, to determine the actual probability of the selecting 1 girl and 2 boys, we do the following:

⇒ number of possible outcomes producing the event × probability of just one outcome

⇒3×1063=3063=1021

While the above method certainly works, there were many steps and calculations that were taken to arrive at the correct answer. Let’s now solve this question using the combination approach. Once again, we will separately determine the favorable outcomes and total outcomes.

“Combination Method” Solution

We need to determine the following:

P(1 girl and 2 boys)=number of ways 1 girl and 2 boys are selectedtotal number of ways 3 students are selected from 9

Let’s start by determining the number of favorable outcomes.

We need to determine the number of ways to select one girl and two boys. We will use the box and fill method:

Number of ways to select 1 girl out of 4:

⇒C41=4

Number of ways to select 2 boys out of 5:

⇒C52=542!=5×42=10 ways

The total number of ways to select the boys and girls is the product of the number of ways that the one girl can be selected and the number of ways that the two boys can be selected, which is 4 × 10 = 40 ways. Thus, the number of favorable outcomes is 40. Next, let’s determine the total number of outcomes.

We can determine the number of ways to select three students from the class of nine. We will use the box and fill method:

⇒C93=9873!=9×8×73×2=3×4×7=84 ways

To determine the final answer, we use the familiar formula:

Probability of an event=number of favorable outcomestotal number of outcomes

Since the number of favorable outcomes is 40 and the total number of outcomes is 84,

the probability of selecting one girl and two boys is 4084=1021.

After comparing the two methods used to solve the problem above, it should be clear that when we used the combination method, we were able to obtain the correct answer with fewer calculations that were also more straightforward.

Text Solution:

We need to determine the following:

⇒Probability of an event=number of favorable outcomestotal number of outcomes⇒P(selecting 2 cats and 3 dogs)=number of ways 2 cats and 3 dogs can be selectednumber of ways 5 animals can be selected from the group

Let’s start by determining the number of favorable outcomes.

We need to determine the number of ways to select three cats from five and two dogs from three. We will use the box and fill method:

Cats: 3 cats are selected from 5 available cats:

⇒C53=5433!=5×4×33×2=5×2=10 ways

Dogs: 2 dogs are selected from 3 available dogs:

⇒C32=322!=3×22=3 ways

The total number of ways to select the dogs and cats is the product of the number of ways that the three cats can be selected and the number of ways that the two dogs can be selected, which is 10 × 3 = 30 ways. Thus, the number of favorable outcomes is 30. Next, let’s determine the total number of outcomes.

We can determine the number of ways to select five animals from the group of eight. We will use the box and fill method:

5 animals are selected from 8 available animals:

⇒C85=876545!=8×7×6×5×45×4×3×2=8×7=56 ways

Since the number of favorable outcomes is 30 and the total number of outcomes is 56, the probability of selecting two cats and three dogs is 3056=1528.

Alternate Solution:

If we wanted to follow the traditional probability approach we could have done the following:

First, we need to determine the number of outcomes. Letting C = a cat is selected and D = a dog is selected, we can determine the number of ways for selecting 3 cats and 2 dogs by determining the number of ways to arrange the letters CCCDD.

CCCDD can be arranged in 5!3!×2!=5×42=10, so there are ten possible orders in which we could select 3 cats and 2 dogs.

Next, we can determine the probability of one possible outcome:

One possible outcome is CCCDD. To determine the probability of that outcome, we can do the following:

P(CCCDD) = 58×47×36×35×24=58×47×12×35×12=601,120=6112

Finally, to determine the actual probability of the selecting 3 cats and 2 dogs, we do the following:

⇒ number of possible outcomes producing the event × probability of just one outcome

⇒10×6112=60112=3056=1528

Why are we considering order?

TTP Assistant

In the alternate method, we first calculate the probability of CCCDD, in that specific order (that is, the first three choices are cats, and the last two choices are dogs).

However, the question is asking for the probability that the selection includes three cats and two dogs, and CCCDD is not the only way the selection can include three cats and two dogs (for instance, we could have DDCCC or CDCDC or CDDCC, etc).

Each of these scenarios has the same probability as CCCDD, so if we knew how many ways the selection can include three cats and two dogs, we would obtain the answer simply by multiplying P(CCCDD) by that number.

At this point, we should notice that every way the selection can include three cats and two dogs is a different arrangement of the letters C, C, C, D, and D.

But in none of these lists do we put the three cats (or the two dogs) in any particular order. The cats are just CCC, and they are not put in any order, as they are considered “indistinguishable” in the problem. In other words, there are just three cats CCC and not C1 and C2 and C3. Thus, we are NOT ordering the three cats. Similarly, we do not order the two dogs; they are just DD and not D1 or D2.

Fortunately, we have an easy formula to determine the number of ways to arrange letters when there are duplicates; that formula is discussed here. Applying that formula, we see that there are 5!/(3! * 2!) = 10 different arrangements of CCCDD, so the answer is simply the product of P(CCCDD) and 10.

Text Solution:

We need to determine the following:

⇒Probability of an event=number of favorable outcomestotal number of outcomes⇒P(2 even cards and 2 odd cards)=number to ways to select 2 even cards and 2 odd cardsnumber of ways to select 4 cards from 11

Let’s start by determining the number of favorable outcomes.

We need to determine the number of ways to select 2 even cards and 2 odd cards. We will use the box and fill method:

Number of ways to select 2 even cards from 4:

⇒C42=432!=4×32=2×3=6 ways

Number of ways to select 2 odd cards from 7:

⇒C72=762!=7×62=7×3=21 ways

The total number of ways to select the cards is the product of the number of ways that the two even cards can be selected and the number of ways that the two odd cards can be selected, which is 6 × 21 = 126 ways. Thus, the number of favorable outcomes is 126. Next, let’s determine the total number of outcomes.

We need to determine the number of ways to select four cards from eleven. We will use the box and fill method:

⇒C114=1110984!=11×10×9×84×3×2=11×10×3=330 ways

Since the number of favorable outcomes is 126 and the total number of outcomes is 330, the probability of selecting two even cards and two odd cards is 126330=2155.

Alternate Solution:

If we wanted to follow the traditional probability approach we could have done the following:

First, we need to determine the number of outcomes. Letting E = an even numbered card is selected and D = an odd numbered card is selected. We can determine the number of ways for selecting 2 even numbered cards and 2 odd numbered cards by determining the number of ways to arrange the letters EEDD.

EEDD can be arranged in 4!2!×2!=3×2=6, so there are six possible orders in which we could select 2 even numbered cards and 2 odd numbered cards.

Next, we can determine the probability of one possible outcome:

One possible outcome is EEDD. To determine the probability of that outcome, we can do the following:

P(EEDD) = 411×310×79×68=111×110×73×62=42660=7110

Finally, to determine the actual probability of the selecting 2 even numbered cards and 2 odd numbered cards, we do the following:

⇒ number of possible outcomes producing the event × probability of just one outcome

⇒6×7110=42110=2155

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
