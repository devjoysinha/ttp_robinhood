19. Functions and Sequences

19.22 Repeating Sequences

Some sequence questions involve sequences with repeating patterns of numbers. When dealing with repeating sequences, its useful to list several terms at the beginning of the sequence to determine the nature of the pattern.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

When dealing with sequences with repeating patterns, list several terms at the beginning of the sequence to determine the nature of the pattern.

Consider, for example, the sequence defined as follows:

The infinite sequence a1, a2, a3, . . . an, . . . is such that an = (-1)n + 5 for all integers n > 0.

So, lets find the first few terms of this sequence:

an=(1)n+5(forall integersn>0)a1=(1)1+5=(1)+5=4a2=(1)2+5=1+5=6a3=(1)3+5=(1)+5=4a4=(1)4+5=1+5=6a5=(1)5+5=(1)+5=4a6=(1)6+5=1+5=6

The first six terms of the sequence are 4, 6, 4, 6, 4, 6, and we can see we have a repeating pattern since the values of the terms alternate between 4 and 6.

We say that the pattern 4-6 repeats every 2 terms, which means the repeating pattern has a length of 2.

In general, we can say that, if a pattern in a repeating sequence repeats itself every r terms, then the repeating pattern has a length of r.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

If a pattern in a repeating sequence repeats itself every r terms, then the repeating pattern has a length of r.

There are two main types of questions involving sequences with repeating patterns:

1. Find a specific term in the sequence.

2. Find the sum of a set of consecutive terms in the sequence.

Lets start with the first question type.

Finding terms in a repeating sequence

The first step in determining the value of a certain term in a repeating sequence is to list several terms at the beginning of the sequence to determine the nature of the repeating pattern.

Consider this mini-example:

The infinite sequence a1, a2, a3, . . . an, . . . is such that a1 = 8, a2 = 5, a3 = 7, a4 = 11, and an = an-4 for all integers n > 4. What is the 83rd term in the sequence?

Notice that the question tells us that an = an-4. This means every term after a4 will be equal to the term thats 4 terms before it, which means we have a repeating sequence.

To better understand the repeating pattern in this sequence, lets calculate several of the terms at the beginning of the sequence:

a1=8(given)a2=5(given)a3=7(given)a4=11(given)an=an-4(forall integersn>4)a5=a5-4=a1=8a6=a6-4=a2=5a7=a7-4=a3=7a8=a8-4=a4=11a9=a9-4=a5=8a10=a10-4=a6=5

So, the first ten terms of the sequence are 8, 5, 7, 11, 8, 5, 7, 11, 8, 5, . . .

Notice that the pattern 8-5-7-11 repeats itself every 4 terms, which means the pattern has a length of 4.

Since the pattern repeats after every 4 terms, and since the 4th term is 11, we know, for example, that a4 = a8 = a12 = a16 = 11. In other words, if k is a positive multiple of 4 (the length of the repeating pattern), then ak = 11.

So, to determine the 83rd term in the sequence, we need only recognize that 80 is the greatest multiple of 4 that is less than 83.

Since 80 is a multiple of 4, we know that a80 = 11.

Lets now continue the 8-5-7-11 pattern until we reach a83.

a80=11a81=8a82=5a83=7

Thus, the 83rd term in the sequence is 7.

An important part of our solution was recognizing that since the pattern repeats after every 4 terms, and since the 4th term is 11, we know that every term number that is a positive multiple of 4 will also be 11. In other words, terms such as a4, a8, a12, and a16 must all be 11.

In general, we can say that if an infinite sequence a1, a2, a3, . . . an, . . . has a repeating pattern of length L and if aL = x, then a2L, a3L, a4L, etc. will also equal x.

t1=6(given)t2=0(given)t3=4(given)tn=tn-3(forall integersn>3)t4=t4-3=t1=6t5=t5-3=t2=0t6=t6-3=t3=4t7=t7-3=t4=6

t63=4t64=6

Thus, the 64th term in the sequence is 6.

Text Solution:

In this question, its not immediately obvious that the defined sequence has a repeating pattern. However, since the first two terms of the sequence are 1 and 3, and since the five answer choices for the value of the 52nd term are very close to the first two terms, theres a good chance that the sequence has a repeating pattern.

To help us spot a pattern, lets determine the first few terms:

a1=1(given)a2=3(given)an=an-1an-2(forall integersn>3)a3=a3-1a3-2=a2a1=31=2a4=a4-1a4-2=a3a2=23=1a5=a5-1a5-2=a4a3=(1)2=3a6=a6-1a6-2=a5a4=(3)(1)=2a7=a7-1a7-2=a6a5=(2)(3)=1a8=a8-1a8-2=a7a6=1(2)=3a9=a9-1a9-2=a8a7=31=2

We can see that the first 9 terms of the sequence are 1, 3, 2, -1, -3, -2, 1, 3, 2.

Notice that the pattern 1,3,2,-1,-3,-2 repeats itself every 6 terms, which means the pattern has a length of 6.

Since the pattern has length 6, and since the 6th term in the sequence is -2, we know for example that a6 = a12 = a18 = a24 = -2. In other words, if k is a positive multiple of 6 (the length of the repeating pattern), then ak = -2.

So, to determine the value of the sequences 52nd term, we need only recognize that 48 is the greatest multiple of 6 that is less than 52.

Since 48 is a multiple of 6, we know that a48 = -2.

Lets now continue the [1,3,2,-1,-3,-2] pattern until we reach a52.

a48=2a49=1a50=3a51=2a52=1

Thus, a52 = -1.

Correct answer: A

Lets now discuss how to find sums of several consecutive terms in repeating sequences.

Finding Sums in Repeating Sequences

Some repeating sequence questions will ask us to find the sum of a set of consecutive terms in a given sequence.

For these questions, we will continue to calculate the first few terms to help us identify the repeated pattern and its length. We will also determine, when possible, some terms at the end of the range we are asked to add.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

When we must find the sum of a set of consecutive values in a repeating sequence, we can gain a deeper understanding of the sum by listing the first and last few terms in the range we are asked to add.

Consider this mini-example:

The infinite sequence a1, a2, a3, . . . an, . . . is such that an = (-1)n. What is the sum of the first 35 terms of the sequence?

To help us spot a pattern, lets determine the first few terms of the sequence:

an=(1)na1=(1)1=1a2=(1)2=1a3=(1)3=1a4=(1)4=1

The first four terms of the sequence are -1, 1, -1 and 1. Notice that the pattern (-1)-1 repeats itself every 2 terms, which means the pattern has a length of 2.

Since the pattern repeats every 2 terms, and since the 2nd term in the sequence is 1, we know, for example, that a2 = a4 = a6 = a8 = 1. In other words, if k is a positive multiple of 2 (the length of the repeating pattern), then ak = 1.

Since were asked for the sum of the first 35 terms of this sequence, lets determine the last few values in that range by starting with the 32nd term. Since 32 is a positive multiple of 2, we know that a32 = 1, and from here we can continue the -1, 1 pattern until we reach a35:

an=(1)na32=1a33=1a34=1a35=1

Note: We could have also used the formula an = (-1)n to determine the values of a32 to a35.

So, our desired sum looks like this:

sumof first 35 terms=a1+a2+a3+a4+. . .+a32+a33+a34+a35sumof first 35 terms=(1)+1+(1)+1+. . .+1+(1)+1+(1)

From here, to determine the sum, recognize that every two consecutive terms in the sequence add to (-1) + 1 = 0, and since the first 35 terms of the sequence can be arranged in 17 pairs of consecutive terms plus one additional term, we get:

sumof first 35 terms=(1)+1+(1)+1+. . .+1+(1)+1+(1)sumof first 35 terms=[(1)+1]+[(1)+1]+. . .[(1)+1]+[(1)+1]+(1)sumof first 35 terms=[0]+[0]+. . .[0]+[0]+(1)sumof first 35 terms=1

Thus, the sum of the first 35 terms is -1.

In most cases, adding the values in one set of repeating terms will help us quickly reach the desired sum.

t1=9(given)t2=2(given)t3=8(given)tn=tn-3(forn>3)t4=t4-3=t1=9t5=t5-3=t2=2t6=t6-3=t3=8

Since the pattern (-9)-2-8 repeats every 3 terms, the pattern has a length of 3.

Since the pattern has length 3, and since the 3rd term in the sequence is 8, we know for example that t3 = t6 = t9 = t12 = 8. In other words, if k is a positive multiple of 3 (the length of the repeating pattern) then tk = 8.

So, since 63 is a multiple of 3, we know that t63 = 8.

t63=8t64=9t65=2

Sum=t1+t2+t3+t4+t5+t6+. . .+t63+t64+t65Sum=(9)+2+8+(9)+2+8+. . .+8+(9)+2

Since the first 65 terms of the sequence can be arranged in 21 sets of 3 consecutive terms plus 2 more terms, we get:

Sum=(9)+2+8+(9)+2+8+. . .+8+(9)+2Sum=[(9)+2+8]+[(9)+2+8]+. . .+[(9)+2+8]+(9)+2Sum=[1]+[1]+. . .[1]+(9)+2Sum=(211)+(9)+2Sum=21+(9)+2=14

To help us spot a pattern, lets determine the first and last few terms of the sequence, beginning with the first few terms:

a1=0(given)a2=2(given)an=an-1an-2(forall integersn>3)a3=a3-1a3-2=a2a1=20=2a4=a4-1a4-2=a3a2=22=0a5=a5-1a5-2=a4a3=02=2a6=a6-1a6-2=a5a4=(2)(0)=2a7=a7-1a7-2=a6a5=(2)(2)=0a8=a8-1a8-2=a7a6=0(2)=2a9=a9-1a9-2=a8a7=20=2a10=a10-1a10-2=a9a8=22=0

So, the first ten terms of the sequence are 0, 2, 2, 0, -2, -2, 0, 2, 2, 0,. . . etc.

Since the pattern 0,2,2,0,-2,-2 repeats every 6 terms, the pattern has a length of 6.

Since the pattern has length 6, and since the 6th term in the sequence is -2, we know for example that t6 = a12 = a18 = a24 = -2. In other words, if k is a positive multiple of 6 (the length of the repeating pattern) then ak = -2.

We want to find the sum of the first 100 terms. To determine the last few terms in this range, we need only recognize that 96 is the greatest multiple of 6 that is less than 100.

So, since 96 is a multiple of 6, we know that a96 = -2.

Lets now continue the [0,2,2,0,-2,-2] pattern until we reach a100.

a96=2a97=0a98=2a99=2a100=0

So, we want to find the following sum:

Sum=a1+a2+a3+a4+a5+a6+a7+a8+a9+a10+. . .+a96+a97+a98+a99+a100Sum=0+2+2+0+(2)+(2)+0+2+2+0+. . .+(2)+0+2+2+0

To find this sum, we can recognize that every six consecutive terms in the pattern add to 0 + 2 + 2 + 0 + (-2) + (-2) = 0.

Since the first 100 terms of the sequence can be arranged in 16 sets of 6 consecutive terms plus 4 more terms, we get:

Sum=a1+a2+a3+a4+a5+a6+a7+a8+a9+a10+. . .+a96+a97+a98+a99+a100Sum=0+2+2+0+(2)+(2)+0+2+2+0+. . .+(2)+0+2+2+0Sum=[0+2+2+0+(2)+(2)]+[0+2+2+0+(2)+(2)]+. . .+[0+2+2+0+(2)+(2)]+0+2+2+0Sum=[0]+[0]+. . .+[0]+0+2+2+0Sum=4

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
