19. Functions and Sequences

19.23.2 Finding the product of a set of consecutive terms in a sequence

The technique for finding the product of a set of consecutive terms in a sequence is virtually the same as that used to calculate the sum of consecutive terms, but there is one notable exception: when finding the sum, we are looking for pairs of values that add to zero, and when finding the product, we are looking for fractions that multiply to one.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

When calculating the product of the terms in a sequence, we are looking for numerators and denominators that cancel out, meaning fractions that multiply to one.

For example, lets say we want to calculate the product 57799111113.

Here, we must resist the temptation to multiply all the numerators and all the denominators and then attempt to simplify the resulting fraction (which, by the way, works out to 3,4659,009 ). Instead, we can notice that many of the values in the numerators also appear in the denominators. In such cases, we can cancel many of the values.

So, for example, we can cancel both the 7 in the denominator of the first fraction and the 7 in the numerator of the second fraction:

product=57799111113product=57799111113

Similarly, we can cancel both the 9 in the denominator of the second fraction and the 9 in the numerator of the third fraction:

product=57799111113product=57799111113

Finally, we can also cancel both the 11 in the denominator of the third fraction and the 11 in the numerator of the fourth fraction, at which point, we can quickly determine the required product, using the only two values that are left:

product=57799111113product=57799111113product=513

We will use similar canceling techniques when finding the product of a set of terms in a sequence.

Consider this mini-example:

The sequence t1, t2, t3, . . . tn, . . . , is such that tn=nn+1 for all integers n 1. What is the product of the first 20 terms of the sequence?

Lets use the formula tn=nn+1 to determine the first and last few terms in the range were considering, beginning with the first few terms:

tn=nn+1for n1t1=11+1=12t2=22+1=23t3=33+1=34t4=44+1=45t5=55+1=56

Since the first five terms are 12,23,34,45,and56, we can see that the sequence does not repeat.

Now lets calculate some of the terms near the end of the required range:

tn=nn+1for n1t18=1818+1=1819t19=1919+1=1920t20=2020+1=2021

So, the required product is as follows:

product=t1t2t3t4t5. . .t18t19t20product=1223344556. . .181919202021

At this point, we can see that many of the values in the numerators also appear in the denominators, which means we can cancel them.

For example, we can cancel the 2 in the denominator of the first fraction and the 2 in the numerator of the second fraction:

product=1223344556. . .181919202021

Continuing the process of canceling numerators and denominators that are equal, we can quickly determine the required product by seeing that we are left with just the first numerator and last denominator.

product=1223344556. . .181919202021product=121

Thus, the product of the first 20 terms is 121.

Lets use the formula tn=n+3n+1 to determine the first and last few terms in the range were considering, beginning with the first few terms:

tn=n+3n+1for n1t1=1+31+1=42t2=2+32+1=53t3=3+33+1=64t4=4+34+1=75t5=5+35+1=86

Since the first five terms are 42,53,64,75,and86, we can see that the sequence does not repeat. Also, notice that we didnt bother to simplify each fraction since doing so would make it harder to spot identical numerators and denominators to cancel.

Now, lets calculate some of the terms near the end of the required range:

tn=n+3n+1for n1t98=98+398+1=10199t99=99+399+1=102100t100=100+3100+1=103101

So, the required product is as follows:

product=t1t2t3t4t5. . .t98t99t100product=4253647586. . .10199102100103101

At this point, we can look for identical numerators and denominators to cancel. Alternatively, we can see that, if we multiply all the numerators and multiply all the denominators we get:

product=45678. . .101102103234567. . .99100101

The numerator is the product of the integers from 4 to 103, inclusive, and the denominator is the product of integers from 2 to 101, inclusive. As such we can cancel any values that appear in both the numerator and denominator, which is all the integers from 4 to 101, inclusive:

product=45678. . .101102103234567. . .99100101product=10210323=17103=1,751

Text Solution:

Lets use the formula tn=2nn+1 to determine the first and last few terms in the range were considering, beginning with the first few terms:

tn=2nn+1for n1t1=211+1=212t2=222+1=223t3=233+1=234t4=244+1=245t5=255+1=256

Since the first five terms are 212,223,234,245,and256, we can see that the sequence does not repeat.

Also, notice that we didnt bother to simplify the numerators just in case its possible to cancel some of the terms.

Now lets calculate some of the terms near the end of the required range:

tn=2nn+1for n1t28=22828+1=22829t29=22929+1=22930t30=23030+1=23031t31=23131+1=23132

So, the required product is as follows:

product=212223234245256. . .22829229302303123132

At this point, we can look for identical numerators and denominators to cancel.

Notice that, except for the last fraction, the value in the denominator of every fraction can also be found in the numerator of the next fraction, where it is being multiplied by 2.

For example, the first fraction has a 2 in the denominator, and in numerator of the second fraction, we are multiplying 2 by 2. Thus, we can cancel both 2s:

product=212223234245256. . .22829229302303123132

Similarly, the second fraction has a 3 in the denominator, and in numerator of the third fraction, we are multiplying 2 by 3. Thus, we can cancel both 3s:

product=212223234245256. . .22829229302303123132

Similarly, the third fraction has a 4 in the denominator, and in numerator of the fourth fraction, we are multiplying 2 by 4. Thus, we can cancel both 4s:

product=212223234245256. . .22829229302303123132

When we continue the process of cancelling, we get:

product=212223234245256. . .22829229302303123132

All that remains now is a 2 in each of the numerators, and just a 32 in the denominator.

Since we are finding the product of 31 terms, we know we must multiply 31 2s in the numerator. We can see the answer choices are expressed as exponents of 2, so we will write this as 231 in order to simplify the fraction further. We can also note that the 32 in the denominator is equal to 25:

product=212223234245256. . .22829229302303123132product=23132product=23125=2315=226

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
