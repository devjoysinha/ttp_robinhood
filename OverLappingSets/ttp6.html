13. Overlapping Sets

13.2.4 Integrating Algebra Into Overlapping Set Problems

Some of the more difficult overlapping set problems may require us to set up and solve one or more algebraic equations or use algebraic expressions when setting up our set-matrix.

When algebra is involved, its especially critical to pay close attention to the language of the question stem, to ensure that we are representing the correct cell with the correct variables.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

When algebra is needed in an overlapping set-matrix, read the given information carefully so that the correct cells are populated with the correct variables.

Here are a few examples:

In a group of people, the number of people who speak both French and Spanish is 4 more than the number of people who speak neither French nor Spanish.

Here, we would let x = number of people in the group who speak neither French nor Spanish; then, x + 4 = number of people in the group who speak both French and Spanish.

In a group of men and women, some who are vegetarians and some who are not, 60 percent of the women in the group are vegetarians.

Here we would let w = the total number of women in the group and 0.6w = the number of women vegetarians in the group.

Lets practice with the following mini-example.

In a group of people, the number of people who own a dog is twice the number of people who own a cat. If 18 of the dog owners own a cat, and 150 cat owners do not own a dog, then how many people own a dog?

If we let x = the total number of people who own a cat, then 2x = the total number of people who own a dog. The set-matrix becomes:

We can assume that there are 100 guests at the dinner party since no specific number of guests is defined. This assumption results in having 40 people wear both jackets and ties. Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
