7. General Word Problems

7.14 Word Problems with Inequalities and Equations

When presented with inequality word problems on the GMAT, we must be able to efficiently do the following:

1) Translate words into inequalities.

2) Deal with inequalities and equations in the same problem.

Let’s begin by discussing the process of translating words into inequalities.

Earlier in the chapter, we discussed how to translate words into equations. The good news is that we use similar skills when we translate words into inequalities. Let’s look at some commonly used words and phrases and whether they translate into either a greater than, less than, greater than or equal to, or less than or equal to sign.

ENGLISH SIGN TRANSLATION
“up to” ≤ “less than or equal to”
“more than” > “greater than”
“at least” ≥ “greater than or equal to”
“exceed” > “greater than”
“no more” ≤ “less than or equal to”
“at most” ≤ “less than or equal to”
“as few as” ≥ “greater than or equal to”

Next, let’s practice translating some phrases into inequalities.

We can use the following variables:

x = the amount of money Carla has

y = the amount of money John has

Example 1:

John has $10 at most.

Translation: y ≤ 10

Example 2:

John has at least as much money as Carla.

Translation: y ≥ x

Example 3:

The amount of money John has is more than the amount of money Carla has.

Translation: y > x

Let’s try a few translations that are more challenging.

Example 4:

The amount of money John has is more than twice the amount of money Carla has.

Translation: y > 2x

Example 5:

John has at least three times as much money as Carla.

Translation: y ≥ 3x

Now that we have practiced translating words into inequalities, we can discuss how to approach and solve problems involving equations and inequalities.

Word Problems with Inequalities and Equations

When solving these problems, we express the inequality in terms of just one variable (ideally the variable we are solving for). This is done by substituting from the equation into the inequality. Let’s review this technique.

For example, let’s say we have the following inequality and equation and would like to express the inequality in terms of m.

n > 2m

and

3m + 2 = n

Since we need to express the inequality in terms of m, we substitute 3m + 2 for n in the inequality n > 2m:

3m + 2 > 2m

m > -2

When we have an equation and an inequality each with the same variables, we can isolate one variable in terms of the other in the equation, and then substitute the isolated variable into the inequality.

Now that we have practiced translating inequalities and substituting from equations into inequalities, we can work through the following example.

Delilah has more than 20 red bottle caps in her bottle cap collection. If she were to gain 20 more blue bottle caps, the number of blue bottle caps in her collection would be 4 more than 2 times the number of red bottle caps. What must be true about the original number of blue bottle caps in her collection?

Let’s first define our variables:

r = the number of red bottle caps

b = the number of blue bottle caps

Next we can create an inequality and an equation from the given information.

“Delilah has more than 20 red bottle caps in her bottle cap collection” translates to:

r > 20

“If she were to gain 20 more blue bottle caps, the number of blue bottle caps in her collection would be 4 more than 2 times the number of red bottle caps” translates to:

b + 20 = 2r + 4

b + 16 = 2r

b+162=r

The final step is to get the inequality in terms of the variable we care about (b). Thus, we substitute b+162 for r into the inequality r > 20:

⇒b+162>20⇒b+16>40⇒b>24

We see that the number of blue bottle caps is greater than 24.

Text Solution:

Question Stem Analysis:

We know that revenue is equal to quantity times unit price. So let m be the number of brand X toothbrushes sold and n be the number of brand Y toothbrushes sold. Therefore, the revenue generated by brand X toothbrushes is m × x and the revenue generated by brand Y toothbrushes is n × y. The question is: m×x > n×y?

Statement One Alone:

⇒ A brand X toothbrush is sold for $1 more than a brand Y toothbrush.

From this, x = y+ 1. Therefore,

⇒m×x > n×y?

⇒m×(y + 1) > n×y?

However, we don’t know anything about m and n. This is not enough to answer the question; statement one alone is not sufficient.

Eliminate answer choices A and D.

Statement Two Alone:

⇒ Ten fewer brand X toothbrushes are sold than brand Y toothbrushes.

From this, m = n – 10. Therefore,

⇒m×x > n×y?

⇒(n – 10)×x > n×y?

However, we don’t know anything about x and y. This is not enough to answer the question; statement two alone is not sufficient.

Eliminate answer choice B.

Statements One and Two Together:

Using statements one and two, we have:

⇒m×x > n×y?

⇒(n – 10)×(y + 1) > n×y?

⇒n×y + n – 10y – 10 > n×y?

⇒n – 10y – 10 > 0?

⇒n > 10y + 10?

However, we don’t know the relationship between n and y. This is not enough to answer the question; statements one and two together are still not sufficient.

Correct answer: E

Hi Joy, do you need a hint for this example?

There are a total of 64 red and green marbles in a jar. If there are fewer than 44 green marbles in the jar, how many green marbles are in the jar?

1) The number of green marbles in the jar is greater than 2 times the number of red marbles in the jar.

2) The number of red marbles in the jar is less than 22.

Statement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
Statement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
BOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
EACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
Statements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.
Confirm your answer
Show solution Hide solution
PRESENTED BY :
Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor

Text Solution:

We can create the following variables:

r = the number of red marbles in the jar

g = the number of green marbles in the jar

We can create the following equation:

r + g = 64

We are also given that g < 44.

We need to determine the value of g.

Statement One Alone:

⇒ The number of green marbles in the jar is greater than 2 times the number of red marbles in the jar.

We can create the following inequality:

⇒g>2r

Since we need to determine g, we can manipulate the given equation:

⇒r=64−g

We can substitute 64 – g into the above inequality:

⇒g>2(64−g)⇒g>128−2g⇒3g>128⇒g>1283=4223

Using the inequality g < 44, we have 4223<g<44. Since g must be an integer, we see that g must be 43. Statement one alone is sufficient.

Statement Two Alone:

⇒ The number of red marbles in the jar is less than 22.

Using the information in statement two, we can create the following inequality:

⇒r<22

Once again, we can use the equation r = 64 – g to get the inequality r < 22 in terms of g. Thus, we will substitute 64 – g for r in the inequality:

⇒64−g<22⇒−g<−42⇒g>42

Thus, 42 < g < 44. Since g must be an integer, we see that g must be 43. Statement two alone is sufficient.

Correct answer: D

Example 66 DI Data Sufficiency

hard

Need a Little Help?
Hi Joy, do you need a hint for this example?

Penny and Nicole collect old pennies and nickels. Penny has more pennies than nickels. Nicole has more nickels than pennies. Who has more coins?

1) The number of pennies Penny has is 1 more than twice the number of nickels Nicole has.

2) The number of pennies Nicole has is 1 more than twice the number of nickels Penny has.

Statement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
Statement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
BOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
EACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
Statements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.
Confirm your answer
Show solution Hide solution
PRESENTED BY :
Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor

Text Solution:

Question Stem Analysis:

First, we define the following variables:

p = number of pennies Penny has

n = number of nickels Penny has

q = number of pennies Nicole has

m = number of nickels Nicole has

We know that p > n and m > q.

Since we need to determine who has more coins, we can translate the question as the following:

Is p + n > q + m?

Statement One Alone:

⇒ The number of pennies Penny has is 1 more than twice the number of nickels Nicole has.

From statement one, we can create the following equation:

p = 2m + 1

Next, we can substitute 2m + 1 for p in the original inequality, and we have the following:

Is 2m + 1 + n > q + m?

Is m + 1 + n > q?

Since we know from the given information that m > q, it must be true that m + 1 + n > q. Thus, we can say that m + 1 IS GREATER than q. Statement one is sufficient to answer the question.

Eliminate answer choices B, C, and E.

Statement Two Alone:

⇒ The number of pennies Nicole has is 1 more than twice the number of nickels Penny has.

From statement two, we can create the following equation:

q = 2n + 1

Next, we can substitute 2n + 1 for q in the original inequality, and we have the following:

Is p + n > 2n + 1 + m?

Is p > n + 1 + m?

Although we know that p > n, we still do not have any information regarding the relationship between p and m, so we do not have enough information to answer the question. Statement two is not sufficient.

Correct answer: A

Frequently Asked Questions
1

I don't understand how, with statement one, we could use d > c when we simplified to d + 1 + b > c. However, we couldn't use a > b when we simplified the second statement to a > b + 1 + d.

TTP Assistant

When a positive number is added to the greater side of an inequality, and the smaller side remains unchanged, then the direction of the inequality will remain the same. From d > c, it follows that d + 1 + b > c.

When a positive number is added to the smaller side of an inequality, and the greater side remains unchanged, then whether the direction of the inequality remains the same depends on the difference between the initial two sides and on the positive number that is added to the smaller side. From a > b, it doesn't necessarily follow that a > b + 1 + d.

Consider the following analogous situation:

If Abraham’s monthly salary is greater than John’s monthly salary and Abraham gets a raise to his monthly salary but John’s monthly salary remains unchanged, then who will have the greater monthly salary, Abraham or John?

Of course, Abraham will have the greater monthly salary because If a > j, then it is true that a + r > j.

On the other hand, if Abraham’s monthly salary is greater than John’s monthly salary and John gets a raise to his monthly salary but Abraham’s monthly salary remains unchanged, then who will have the greater monthly salary, Abraham or John?

The thing is we can’t tell! The answer depends on the difference between their initial monthly salaries and the amount of the raise that John gets. If Abraham’s initial monthly salary was $500 greater than John’s and if John gets a $400 raise, then Abraham’s monthly salary will remain higher. Whereas, if Abraham’s initial monthly salary was only $300 greater than John’s and if John gets a $400 raise, then John’s monthly salary will be greater.

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
