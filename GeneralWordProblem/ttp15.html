7. General Word Problems

7.3.2 Word Problems with Differing Unit Prices and Quantities

Some GMAT questions involve considering the unit prices of multiple different items available for purchase. In such questions, we may need to determine one of the unit prices, how many of each item can be purchased with a given amount of money, or some other related value.

To answer questions of this type, we will rely on our translation skills and apply one or more of the following formulas:

totalcost=(cost per item)(# of items purchased)

cost per item=totalcost# of items purchased

# of items purchased=totalcostpriceper item

Lets practice with the following mini-example.

A watermelon at store A costs 2 dollars more than a watermelon at store B. If the number of watermelons one can buy with $120 at store B is 10 greater than the number of watermelons one can buy with $120 at store A, what is the price per watermelon at store B.

We can determine the number of watermelons that can be purchased for $120 at each store using the formula # of items purchased=totalcostpriceper item.

Since we dont know the price per watermelon at each store, lets let x = the price per watermelon (in dollars) at store B.

Since we are told a watermelon at store A costs 2 dollars more than a watermelon at store B, we can say that x + 2 = the price per watermelon (in dollars) at store A.

We can now write expressions to represent the number of watermelons that can be purchased for $120 at each store:

Store A: # of watermelons=totalcostpriceper item=120x+2

Store B: # of watermelons=totalcostpriceper item=120x

Since the number of watermelons one can buy with $120 at store B is 10 greater than the number of watermelons one can buy with $120 at store A, we can write and solve the following equation:

# of watermelons for $120 at store B=(# of watermelons for $120 at store A)+10120x=120x+2+10120x120x+2=10(x)(x+2)(120x120x+2)=10(x)(x+2)120(x+2)120(x)=10x2+20x120x+240120x=10x2+20x240=10x2+20x10x2+20x240=0

You will find that most questions of this type result in a quadratic equation. While the numbers in this equation may seem unwieldy, fortunately, we can solve the equation more easily if we first divide both sides by 10:

10x2+20x240=010x2+20x24010=010x2+2x24=0(x+6)(x4)=0x=6orx=4

Since the price per watermelon cannot be negative, we can eliminate the solution x = -6, which means watermelons cost 4 dollars each at store B. Furthermore, because watermelons at store A cost 2 dollars more than watermelons at store B, we know that watermelons cost 4 + 2 = 6 dollars each at store A.

Text Solution:

Since we are comparing the number of hats that can be purchased for $200 at two different unit prices, we will apply the formula, # of items purchased=totalcostpriceper item.

If we let c = the current price per hat (in dollars) at Hat Mart, then we can also say that c + 5 = the hypothetical price (in dollars) per hat after a 5-dollar increase.

Now lets create algebraic expressions for the number of hats that can be purchased for $200 at the two different unit prices:

Current unit price: # of hats=totalcostpriceper item=200c

Hypothetical unit price: # of hats=totalcostpriceper item=200c+5

Since the number of hats one can buy with $200 at the hypothetical unit price is 2 fewer than the number of hats one can buy with $200 at the current unit price, we can write and solve the following equation:

# of hats for $200 at hypothetical unit price=(# of hats for $200 at current unit price)2200c+5=200c2(c)(c+5)(200c+5)=(200c2)(c)(c+5)200(c)=200(c+5)(2c)(c+5)200c=200c+1,000(2c2+10c)0=1,0002c210c2c2+10c1,000=0

Now lets divide both sides of the equation by 2 and solve:

2c2+10c1,000=0c2+5c500=0

Note: Keep in mind that, if you are having difficulties factoring this expression, you have two alternatives:

1. Apply the quadratic formula.

2. Test each answer choice until you find the one that satisfies the equation.

When we solve the quadratic by factoring we get:

c2+5c500=0(c+25)(c20)=0c=25orc=20

Since the price per hat cannot be negative, we can eliminate the solution c = 25, which means c = 20. So, each hat at Hat Mart currently costs $20.

Correct answer: C

In questions involving differing unit prices, were not always considering prices for discrete objects such as watermelons or hats. Instead, we could also consider unit prices of certain quantities, like the price per kilogram of nuts or the price per hour of work performed.

Fortunately, we can solve these questions by applying the same strategies we used for the earlier examples. We may just need to adapt the language in the formulas we use. For instance, if were trying to find a workers hourly pay rate, we could use the following formula:

pay rate per hour=totaldollars earnedhoursworked

Notice that in this formula the pay rate per hour takes the place of the unit price per item from our earlier examples.

Text Solution:

We are comparing two different wages that are created by working for two different sets of time while being paid the same amount. Thus, we can use the formula:

pay rate per hour=totaldollars earnedhoursworked

We can let h = the number of hours Jackson planned to work. Since Jackson worked 2 fewer hours than he expected, this means that h 2 = the actual number of hours he worked.

Now lets create algebraic expressions for the hourly pay rates for the two different number of hours worked.

Pay rate for planned number of hours:

pay rate per hour=totaldollars earnedhoursworked=280h

Pay rate for actual number of hours:

payrate per hour=totaldollars earnedhoursworked=280h2

Since Jacksons hourly pay rate when he worked h 2 hours was $7 more than if he had worked h hours, we can create the following equation:

pay rate for actual number of hours=pay rate for planned number of hours+7280h2=280h+7

We can multiply both sides of the equation by the LCM of the denominators, which is (h)(h2). So, we have:

(h)(h2)(280h2)=(280h+7)(h)(h2)280(h)(h2)h2=280(h)(h2)h+7(h)(h2)280h=280(h2)+7h(h2)280h=280h560+7h214h0=7h214h560

Now lets divide both sides of the equation by 7 and solve for h:

7h214h5607=07h22h80=0(h10)(h+8)=0h=10orh=8

Since h must be positive, h = 10. Therefore Jackson was supposed to work for 10 hours but actually worked for 10 2 = 8 hours.

Correct answer: A

Need a Little Help?
Hi Joy, do you need a hint for this example?

Hanna drops off her laundry at Hanks Cleaners every Tuesday. For each visit, she spends 20 dollars to get her laundry washed and folded based on a fixed price per pound. However, one week she decided to instead drop the clothes at Mikes Cleaners, where the price per pound was $0.50 less, so she was able to get 2 more pounds of laundry washed and folded for $20. How many pounds of laundry did Hanna get washed and folded at Mikes Cleaners?

2.5

40

20

8

10
Confirm your answer
Show solution Hide solution
PRESENTED BY :
Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor Jeff Miller COO & Expert GMAT Instructor

Text Solution:

We are comparing two different prices per pound for wash-and-fold laundry service when Hanna spends $20 dollars. Thus, we can use the formula:

price per pound=totaldollars spentnumberof pounds

Lets create some variables. We can let p = the number of pounds of laundry that can be washed and folded at Hanks Cleaners for $20. Then, because Hanna was able to wash 2 additional pounds at Mikes for the same $20, we can set p + 2 = the number of pounds of laundry that can be washed and folded at Mikes cleaners for $20.

Now lets create algebraic expressions for the price per pound charged for Hanks and Mikes Cleaners.

Price per pound for Hanks Cleaners: totaldollars spentnumberof pounds=20p

Price per pound for Mikes Cleaners: totaldollars spentnumberof pounds=20p+2

Since price per pound at Mikes Cleaners is $0.50 less than the price per pound at Hanks cleaners, we can create the following equation:

Price per pound for Hank's Cleaners=Price per pound for Mike's Cleaners+0.520p=20p+2+0.5

We can multiply the entire equation by the LCM of the denominators, which is (p)(p+2). So, we have:

(p)(p+2)(20p)=(20p+2+0.5)(p)(p+2)(p)(p+2)20p=(p)(p+2)20p+2+(p)(p+2)(0.5)20p+40=20p+0.5p2+p0=0.5p2+p40=00=p2+2p80=0(p8)(p+10)=0p=8orp=10

Because p represents an amount of laundry, it cannot be negative. Thus, we know that 8 pounds of laundry can be washed and folded at Hanks Cleaners for $20, and so Hanna was able to instead spend her $20 to have 8 + 2 = 10 pounds of laundry washed and folded at Mikes Cleaners.

Correct answer: E

1

Why are we adding instead of subtracting 0.5?

TTP Assistant

We could certainly represent it as a subtraction. The price per pound at Hank's is $.50 more than the price per pound at Mike's, so we would have:

Price(Hank's) - 0.5 = Price(Mike's)

We just have to make sure to put it on the other side of the equation.

2

I set up the problem as 20 / (x - 0.5) - 20/x = 2, where x is the price before reduction. Is this an acceptable way to get the correct answer?

TTP Assistant

Your logic works well:

20 / (x - 0.5) - 20/x = 2

x = 2.5

Since 20/2.5 = 8, we know that she had 8 pounds of laundry done at Hanks. And at Mikes, she was able to get 2 extra pounds done for the same cost. Thus, at Mikes, she got 8 + 2 = 10 pounds of laundry done.

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
