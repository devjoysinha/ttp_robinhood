2.9 Base fractions and their decimal and percent equivalents

As a general rule of thumb, we like to use fractions more than decimals, so we take the opportunity to convert a decimal to a fraction when it appears that the calculation will be easier by doing so. Sometimes when we convert a fraction to a decimal (or a decimal to a fraction), the result is exact. For example, 12 can be precisely expressed as 0.5 (or 0.5 can be precisely expressed as 12). Other times the conversion will be an approximation. For example, 19 can be approximated as 0.111 (or 0.111 can be approximated by 19).

Every fraction has what well refer to as a base fraction. For example, lets look at fractions with a denominator of eight. Of the fractions with a denominator of 8, 18 is the base fraction, which equals 0.125. Every other fraction with a denominator of eight will be a multiple of this base fraction. Consider the following.

18=0.1251=0.12528=0.1252=0.2538=0.1253=0.37548=0.1254=0.5

This understanding allows us to quickly convert a fraction into its decimal form. The elegance is that this strategy can be used with any fraction. For example, what is the decimal approximation of 59?  Since 190.111 (see table below), where the sign means approximately equal to, we can choose 0.111 as the decimal approximation of 19. Then 5950.111=0.555. However, when 59 is expressed as a decimal, it is really 0.5555 with infinitely many 5s right after the decimal point. We see that 0.5555 can be rounded to 0.556. Hence we choose 0.556 (instead of 0.555) as the decimal equivalent of 59.

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.

About Hard Tests

The questions in this Hard Chapter Test are as difficult as any that appear on the GMAT. So, at this stage of your preparation, it does not make sense to seek answer them in under two minutes.

Rather, for best results, you should hide the timer and answer these questions untimed, taking as much time as you need to fully understand and analyze each question.

By practicing in this way, you will give yourself time to learn to see whats going on in the questions and to learn to work your way to correct answers to difficult questions.
