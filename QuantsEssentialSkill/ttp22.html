2. Essential GMAT Quant Skills

2.5 Rounding Numbers

On the GMAT, we must be able to round two different types of numbers.

1. Integers (rounding to the left of the decimal point)

2. Decimals (rounding to the right of the decimal point)

However, before we get into rounding numbers, it’s useful to do a quick review of digits.

Earlier in the chapter, we learned that a number has place values called the ones (or units), tens, hundreds, thousands, and so on. All of these place values are to the left of the decimal point. In addition, every number has a decimal point to the right of the ones place, whether we write that decimal point or not. For example, “9,467” could be written “9,467. ”. If we count to the right of the decimal point, the place values are called the tenths, hundredths, thousandths, and so on.

See the following chart:

Thousands Hundreds Tens Ones (Units) Decimal Point Tenths Hundredths Thousandths
1,000 100 10 1 . 0.1 0.01 0.001

The individual numbers in each place value are called digits. For example:

In the number 9,467, 9 is the thousands digit, 4 is the hundreds digit, 6 is the tens digit, and 7 is the ones digit.

In the number 0.125, 0 is the ones digit, 1 is the tenths digit, 2 is the hundredths digit, and 5 is the thousandths digit.

Knowing the names and locations of these digits is important when we round numbers. Let’s discuss rounding numbers now. We will begin with rounding integers greater than zero, meaning that we’ll be rounding to the left of the decimal point.

Rounding Positive Integers (or Rounding to the Left of the Decimal Point)

Often, we will be asked to round numbers to nearest place values. For example, we might be asked the following:

What is 123 rounded to the nearest ten?

Note that 123 is between two numbers that are “tens,” meaning their ones digit is zero. Specifically, 123 is between 120 and 130. If we are asked which of these 123 is nearest to, then we can say it is nearest to 120, since it is only 3 units away from 120, while it is 7 units away from 130. Therefore, 123 rounded to the nearest ten is 120. Let’s consider another example:

What is 153 rounded to the nearest hundred?

In this case, the “hundreds” that 153 lies between are 100 and 200. We can also see that 153 is nearest to 200, as it is only 47 units from 200, while it is 53 units from 100. Thus, 153 rounded to the nearest hundred is 200. Let’s look at one more example, and then some patterns may start to emerge.

What is 2,764 rounded to the nearest thousand?

The number 2,764 is between the “thousands” 2,000 and 3,000, and of these two, it is nearest to 3,000. (Note that 2,764 – 2,000 = 764, while 3,000 – 2,764 = 236. But even without doing those precise calculations, we can estimate that 2,764 will be closer to 3,000 than to 2,000. ) Therefore, 2,764 rounded to the nearest thousand is 3,000.

Considering the above three examples, we may notice some patterns that will give us the following shortcuts for rounding:

If the number to be rounded is a positive integer, and we want to round it to a certain place value to the left of the decimal point, look at the digit immediately to the right of that place value.

If digit to the right is 0, 1, 2, 3, or 4, we round down by keeping the digit in the original place value the same, and we change all the digits to the right of that place value to zero. For instance, because the ones place of 123 is 3, we rounded 123 down to 120 by keeping the tens place the same and changing the ones place to 0.

However, if the digit to the right is 5, 6, 7, 8, or 9, then we round up by adding 1 to the digit in the original place value, and we change all the digits to the right of that place value to zero. For example, because the tens place of 153 is 5, we rounded 153 up to 200 by changing the 1 in the hundreds place to 2 and changing all the digits to the right to the right of the hundreds digit to 0. Similarly, we rounded 2,764 up to 3,000 because the hundreds place in 2,764 is 7.

Text Solution:

Let's note the values we are given:

First-time artist painting: $974

Seasoned painter average: $4,545

We need to find the difference ("how many dollars less") between these two prices; to do this, we perform subtraction (do not round until the very last step!):

$4,545 – $974 = $3,571

Next, we need to round our result to the hundreds place. Currently, the digit 5 is in the hundreds place. So, we now determine whether we round up or down.

To decide this, we should look at the digit to the right of the 5, which is 7. Since 7 is a number 5 or greater, we should round up. In other words, we add 1 to the digit in the hundreds place. Thus, the 5 becomes a 6, and our number becomes 3,600.

Notice that we deliberately did not round the numbers before performing the subtraction. If we had done so, we would have rounded $4,545 to $4,500 and rounded $974 to $1,000. Then, the result of the rounded subtraction would have been $4,500 – $1,000 = $3,500, which is among our answer choices. However, since we know the actual difference is $3,571, which rounds up to the nearest hundred, $3,500 is not as good an estimate as $3,600. This is why we should only round our answer after performing any operations.

Next, let’s discuss rounding decimals.

Rounding Decimals (or Rounding to the Right of the Decimal Point)

When rounding decimals to a certain place value to the right of the decimal point, there is a method we can follow that is similar to the method we used to round integers. We can begin by examining the digit immediately to the right of the place value we’re rounding to.

If that digit to the right is 0, 1, 2, 3, or 4, we round down by keeping the digit in the original place value the same, and then dropping all the digits to the right of it.

If the digit to the right is 5, 6, 7, 8, or 9, we round up by adding 1 to the digit in the original place value, and then dropping all the digits to the right of it.

Let’s practice with a few examples:

What is 0.14 rounded to the nearest tenth?

Note that the digit in the tenths place is 1. Directly to the right of this is a 4 in the hundredths place. This means we round down by leaving 1 alone and removing all the digits to the right of it. Thus, 0.14 rounded to the nearest tenth is 0.1.

What is 0.179 rounded to the nearest hundredth?

Note that the digit in the hundredths place is 7. Directly to the right of this is a 9 in the thousandths place. This means we round up by adding 1 to 7 to make 8 in the hundredths place, and then we remove the digits to the right of the 8. Thus, 0.179 rounded to the nearest hundredth is 0.18.

What is 0.343 rounded to the nearest tenth?

Note that the digit in the tenths place is 3. Directly to the right of this is a 4 in the hundredths place. This means we round down by leaving 3 alone and removing all the digits to the right of 3. Thus, 0.343 rounded to the nearest tenth is 0.3.

If the digit to the right is 0, 1, 2, 3, or 4, we round down by keeping the digit in the original place value the same, and then dropping all the digits to the right of it.

If the digit to the right is 5, 6, 7, 8, or 9, we round up by adding 1 to the digit in the original place value, and then dropping all the digits to the right of it.

If we want to round 9.746 to the nearest tenth, the digit one place to the right of the decimal point (9. 746), we examine the digit immediately to the right of the tenths place (9.746). Since the digit 4 is less than 5, we keep the 9 and 7, but we drop the 4 and 6. Thus, 9.746 becomes 9.7 when rounded to the nearest tenth.

To round 9.746 to the nearest hundredth, the digit two places to the right of the decimal point (9.746), we examine the digit immediately to the right of the hundredths place (9.746). Answer E also is incorrect, since x < y, and thus xy must be less than 1, not greater than 1. Thus, answer D is correct.

Next, let’s discuss the special case of rounding up when the original digit is 9.

Rounding Up When the Original Digit Is 9

Whether we are rounding decimals or positive integers, when we round up, we add 1 to the original digit in the place value. This method is straightforward when the original digit is 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, or 8. However, when the original digit is 9, adding 1 to 9 produces 10, which is not a single digit. So, you might wonder in which place values to put the digits 1 and 0. In this case, we have to take an extra step when rounding: instead of adding 1 to the original digit, we make it a 0 and add 1 to the digit to the left of that place value.

Let’s practice with a few examples:

What is 195 rounded to the nearest ten?

First, note that the digit in the tens place of 195 is 9 (195). Next, we find the digit directly to the right of the tens place, which we see is 5 (195). A 5 indicates that we round up by adding 1 to 9. Since this would produce 10, we instead make the 9 a 0 and add 1 to the digit to the left of the 0. Thus the 1 in the hundreds place becomes 2. Then we make every digit to the right of the tens place a zero as well. Thus, 195, when rounded to the nearest ten, rounds up to 200.

What is 0.397 rounded to the nearest hundredth?

First, note that digit in the hundredths place of 0.397 is 9 (0.397). Next, we find the digit directly to the right of the hundredths place, which we see is 7 (.0397). A 7 indicates that we round up by adding 1 to 9. Since this would produce 10, we instead make the 9 a 0 and add 1 to the digit to the left of the 0. Thus the 3 in the tenths place becomes 4. Then we remove the digits to the right of the hundredths place. Thus, 0.397 rounded to the nearest hundredth rounds up to 0.40.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

When rounding decimals or positive integers to a place value in which the digit is 9, because adding 1 to 9 produces 10, we have to take an extra step when rounding. Instead of adding 1 to the original digit, we make it a 0 and add 1 to the digit to the left of that place value.

Hi Joy, do you need a hint for this example?

If x = 0.56p7, which of the following must be true?

I. If x were rounded to the nearest tenth, the new number would be 0.6.

II. If x were rounded to the nearest hundredth, the new number would be 0.57.

III. If x were rounded to the nearest thousandth, the new number would be 0.568.

I only

I and II

I and III

II and III

I, II, and III
Confirm your answer
Show solution Hide solution
PRESENTED BY :
Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor

Text Solution:

Statement I:

When rounding to the nearest tenth, we look at the hundredths digit. Since the hundredths digit is greater than equal to 5, we adding 1 to the tenths digit and we drop all the digits to the right. Thus, we obtain 0.6. Statement I is correct.

Statement II:

When rounding to the nearest hundredth, we look at the thousandths digit. We see that the thousandths digit is p, the value of which is unknown. If p is 5 or greater, then we would add 1 to the hundredths digit and drop all the digits to the right to obtain 0.57. However, if p is 4 or less, then we keep the hundredths digit and drop all the digits to the right to obtain 0.56. We see that statement II is not necessarily correct.

Statement III:

When rounding to the nearest thousandth, we look at the ten thousandths digit. Since the ten thousandths digit is 7, we increase the value of the thousandths digit by 1 and drop all the digits to the right. However, the thousandths digit is p and we don’t know the value of p. If p = 7, then x rounded to the nearest thousandth is indeed 0.568; however, if p is anything other than 7, then x rounded to the nearest thousandth will not be 0.568. Statement III is not necessarily correct.

Correct answer: A

Text Solution:

When rounding the decimal A to the nearest hundredth, we look at the thousandths digit. Since the thousandths digit is 9, the hundredths digit will be increased by 1 and all the digits to the right will be dropped. We are told that the result is 7.x3, so the hundredths digit became 3 after it was increased by 1. It follows that the original value of the hundredths digit was 2. Thus, y = 2.

Substituting y = 2 in the decimal A, we obtain A = 7.x29. To round this decimal to the nearest tenth, we look at the hundredths digit. Since the hundredths digit is less than 5, we keep the tenths digit and drop all the digits to the right. We are told that the result is 7.7. Since the value of the tenths digit remained the same after rounding, it follows that the original value of the tenths digit was also 7. Thus, x = 7, and x + y = 7 + 2 = 9.
