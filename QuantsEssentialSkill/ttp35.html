2.10.4 Properties of Square Roots

In this lesson, we will cover some basic properties of square roots that will help us prepare for some of the questions in the next chapter, Linear and Quadratic Equations.

The topics we will cover are:

Squaring a square root

Multiplying two square roots

Multiplying many square roots

Rewriting a single square root as the product of two or more square roots

Different ways to express the same square root

Later, in the Roots and Exponents chapter, we will revisit the concepts taught here while also learning more advanced concepts involving roots and exponents.

Squaring a Square Root

Lets see what happens if we square the square root of a number with a few examples:

(9)2=(3)2=33=9

If we take the square root of 9, we get 3. If we then square 3, the final result is 9.

(49)2=(7)2=77=49

Similarly, if we take the square root of 49 and then square the result, we return to 49.

Theres a general pattern we can observe in the above examples: if we start with a number greater than or equal to 0, take its square root, and then square the result, we will return to the number we started with.

We can summarize this property more succinctly as follows: If n is a nonnegative number, then (n)2=n.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

If n is a nonnegative number, then (n)2=n.

This should make sense because n denotes some number that, when squared, produces the value n. So, when we take that number and square it, we should get n.

Its worth noting that the property also works when the number under the square root sign is not a perfect square such as 4, 25, 49, or 100. For example, when we evaluate 5, we get approximately 2.23607 when rounded to the nearest one hundred thousandth, and when we square 2.23607, we get approximately 5. If we dont round the values, we can say with certainty that (5)2=5.

Lets reinforce this first property with a practice question.

Multiplying Two Square Roots

Lets now see what happens when we multiply two different square roots by multiplying the square root of 4 by the square root of 9:

49=23=6

In this case, we found the square roots of 4 and 9 separately and then multiplied the results. Lets see what happens if we write this multiplication slightly differently, with a larger square root symbol over the entire product:

49=36=6

In this formatting, we must first multiply 4 and 9 to get 36 and then take the square root.

Notice, though, that in both sets of calculations above, we reached the same answer, 6. In other words, we have discovered that 49=49.

This hints at another general property of square roots: the product of the square roots of two numbers is equal to the square root of the product of those two numbers. Or, put more succinctly, if a and b are nonnegative numbers, then ab=ab.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

If a and b are nonnegative numbers, then ab=ab.

The property is especially useful when we must evaluate the product of two square roots when each square root on its own does not produce an integer.

Consider this mini-example:

What is the value of 520?

Since we wont have access to a calculator during the quant section of the GMAT, it would be very hard to individually evaluate 5 and 20. Fortunately, we dont have to. Instead, if we apply the property we discovered above, we can evaluate 520 in our head:

ab=ab520=520520=100520=10

We can extend the property for multiplying two square roots to multiplying more than two square roots:

If a, b, c, d,. . . etc. are nonnegative numbers, then abcd. . .=abcd. . ..

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

If a, b, c, d, . . . etc. are nonnegative numbers, then abcd. . .=abcd. . ..

So, for example, we can calculate 234530 as follows:

234530=234530234530=3,600234530=60

Rewriting a single square root as the product of two or more square roots.

Its important to recognize that the property abcd. . .=abcd. . . also works in the other direction. In other words, abcd. . .=abcd. . ..

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

If a, b, c, d, . . . etc. are nonnegative numbers, then abcd. . .=abcd. . ..

This form of the property can come in handy if we need to find the square root of a large number. In many cases, we can make the calculation easier by factoring the number and splitting the square root into the square roots of several smaller numbers.

Consider this mini-example:

What is the value of 22,500?

Many students will have difficulty thinking of a number that, when squared, equals 22,500. However, if we notice that 22,500 = 100 225, then we can apply the above property to calculate 22,500. Applying the above property, we:

22,500=10022522,500=10022522,500=101522,500=150

However, the above calculation relies on knowing that the square root of 225 is 15. If we dont have that fact memorized, we can still find the answer if we recognize that 225 = 9 25 and apply the same property one more time:

22,500=10022522,500=10022522,500=1092522,500=1092522,500=103522,500=150

The property we applied above also suggests that there can be many ways to express the same square root.

For example, since 100 = 10 10, we know that 100=10, but this is just one way to express 100.

If we recognize that 100 = 4 25, we can write:

100=425100=225100=225

Important: The notation 225 means 225.

Even though we could evaluate 25 if we wanted to, the truth remains that 100 and 225 have equivalent values.

Alternatively, since 100 = 2 50, we can also express 100 as follows:

100=250100=250

This tells us that 100 and 250 have equivalent values.

In some questions, we might be given several different products of square roots to compare, and we will need to use the properties we learned in this chapter to decide which of the expressions are equivalent.

Consider this mini-example:

Which of the following is equivalent to 64?

(A)44(B)28(C)228(D)232(E)6464

We know that 64=8, so we can manipulate each of the expressions in the answer choice to see which of them is equivalent to 64 or 8. In each case, we will apply the properties we observed in this chapter, including the fact that the product of any two square roots is equivalent to the square root of their product.

(A)44=44=16=4

For this expression, we get 4, not 8. So, we can eliminate choice A.

Its worth noticing that we also could have simplified this expression using the property we learned in the previous section: squaring a square root will give back the number we started with: 44=(4)2=4.

(B)28=242=242=222=42

While we can write this square root expression in an equivalent form, it is not equivalent to 8. We can eliminate choice B.

(C)228=228=28

This expression is equivalent to the previous one, and so is a non-integer, not 8.

(D)232=232=64=8

This expression is equivalent to 64 and so equals 8. While weve found the correct answer, we can check the last answer choice to be sure.

(E)6464=(64)2=64

This expression equals 64, which is not equivalent to the square root of 64. We can eliminate choice E.

Thus, the correct expression that is equivalent to 64 is D, 232.

Text Solution:

Lets examine each answer choice separately. We can first notice that several of the answer choices include products of square roots, and each of those expressions will be equivalent to ones in which we first multiply the numbers under one square root. We will use this fact to check which of the expressions is equivalent to 800.

(A) 1080

1080=10801080=800

Thus, 1080 and 800 have equivalent values.

(B) 1650

1650=16501650=800

Thus, 1650 and 800 also have equivalent values.

(C) 202

Since 202 is not expressed as the product of two square roots, we cant apply the same square root property we used for choices A and B.

This time, we will see if its possible to express 800 equivalently as 202.

If we recognize that 800 = 400 2, we can split the square root of 800 into the product of these two smaller square roots:

800=4002800=4002800=202800=202

Thus, 202 and 800 have equivalent values.

(D) 80100

80100=8010080100=8,000

We have found that 80100=8,000. Even without calculating 8,000, we know that it would be much larger than 800. Thus, we can conclude that 80100 and 800 do not have equivalent values.

Although we have just seen that the correct answer is D, lets confirm that choice E is not correct.

(E) 108

If we recognize that 800 = 100 8, we can split 800 into the product of two smaller radicals:

800=1008800=1008800=108800=108

Thus, 108 and 800 have equivalent values.

Correct answer: D

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
