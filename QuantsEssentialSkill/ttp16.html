2.2.4 Using a common numerator to compare the size of fractions

In the previous lesson, we learned about how using a common denominator can help us compare the relative size of two or more fractions. We can also follow a similar process when using common numerators. Doing so is possible because, in a set of positive fractions, if all of the fractions share a common numerator, the larger the denominator, the smaller the fraction, and the smaller the denominator, the larger the fraction.

In a set of positive fractions, if all of the fractions share a common numerator, the larger the denominator, the smaller the fraction, and the smaller the denominator, the larger the fraction.

Let’s practice this idea with the following set of fractions: 417,849,1275

An easy way to make all three of the numerators the same is to transform each of those numerators into the least common numerator of the fractions in the set. In other words, we find the smallest number that is divisible by 4, 8, and 12. An easy method for finding the least common numerator is to list out the multiples of the largest numerator until we find a number that is divisible by all of the numerators in the set.

In these fractions, the largest numerator is 12, so list out the multiples of 12, seeking the first multiple of 12 that is also divisible by 4 and 8:

12, 24

We can stop at 24 because 24 is the first number that is divisible by all of the numerators in the set. In other words, 24 is the first number that is divisible by 4, 8, and 12.

Let’s now manipulate our fractions, so we have a common numerator of 24.

⇒(417)66=24102

⇒(849)33=24147

⇒(1275)22=24150

Ordering the fractions from least to greatest, we have:

⇒24150,24147,24102→1275,849,417.

Notice that, by getting a common numerator, it’s easy to arrange these fractions from least to greatest.

Also, if you were wondering why we went the route of using the least common numerator, instead of the least common denominator, when we compared these fractions, notice that it was much easier to determine the least common numerator of 12, 8, and 4 than it would have been to determine the least common denominator of 75, 49, and 17. So, when tasked with finding the relative size of two or more fractions, depending on whether the numerators or denominators are easier to work with, you’ll know whether to use least common numerator or least common denominator.

Let’s now practice using the least common numerator with a few examples.

Text Solution:

As we learned earlier in the section, when comparing the relative size of two or more fractions, it’s helpful to transform the numerators of those fractions into a common numerator, because in a set of positive fractions, if all of the fractions share a common numerator, the smaller the denominator, the larger the fraction. It’s also worth nothing that we are using common numerator here, as opposed to common denominator, because it’s much easier to get a common numerator for 4, 9, 12, and 18 than it is to get a common denominator for 7, 11, 17, and 23.

We now know that an easy way to make all five of the numerators the same is to transform each of those numerators into the least common numerator of the fractions in the set. In other words, we find the smallest number that is divisible by 4, 9, 12, and 18. An easy method for finding the least common numerator is to list out the multiples of the largest numerator until we find a number that is divisible by all of the numerators in the set.

Since 18 is the largest numerator, we will list out the multiples of 18:

18, 36

We can stop at 36 because 36 is the first number that is divisible by all of the numerators in the set. In other words, 36 is the first number that is divisible by 4, 9, 12, and 18.

Let’s now transform these fractions into fractions that have numerators of 36.

4×97×9=3663 9×411×4=3644 12×317×3=3651 18×223×2=3646

We must remember that the fraction with the smallest denominator is the largest. Thus, the correct ordering of the fractions is 3663<3651<3646<3644 . Replacing each fraction with its reduced form, we obtain 47<1217<1823<911 .

Correct answer: B

Text Solution:

Let’s begin by listing the fractions 67, 1823, 2731 from the smallest to the largest. To determine the relative size of these fractions, we can transform the numerators of the fractions into a common numerator, because in a set of positive fractions, if all of the fractions share a common numerator, the smaller the denominator, the larger the fraction.

As we have learned, to determine the common numerator, we need to find the smallest number divisible by 6, 18, and 27. An easy method for doing so is to list out the multiples of the largest numerator until we find a number that is divisible by all of the numerators in the set. So, in this case, we will list out the multiples of 27 until we find a number that is divisible by 6, 18, and 27.

27, 54

We can stop at 54 because 54 is the first number that is divisible by all of the numerators in the set. In other words, 54 is the first number that is divisible by 6, 18, and 27. Let’s now transform each of the numerators into a numerator of 54.

6×97×9=5463 18×323×3=5469 27×231×2=5462

Since the fraction with the smallest denominator is the largest fraction, these fractions can be ordered as 5469<5463<5462. Since we are told that x is the second smallest fraction, x must be between 5469 and 5463.

Next, let’s express the fraction in every Roman numeral with a numerator of 54.

I. (2732)22=5464

II. (910)66=5460

III. (911)66=5466

We see that if x=5464 or x=5466, then x is between 5469 and 5463. However, if x=5460, then x is greater than 5463, and in that case, x is not the second smallest element in the list. Therefore, the fractions in the Roman numerals I and III can be the value of x.

Correct answer: D

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
