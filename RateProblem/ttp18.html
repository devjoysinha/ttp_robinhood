9. Rate Problems

9.12 Round-Trip Rate Questions

The round-trip problem involves an object going to a destination along some path and then returning from that destination to its starting point along the exact same path. Therefore, the distance from the starting point to the destination equals the distance from the destination back to the starting point.

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

In a round-trip problem, the distance an object travels from the starting point to the destination equals the distance the object travels back from the destination to the starting point.

So, the equation that we generally use in a roundtrip rate problem is Distance 1 = Distance 2
 

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

Since the two distances in a roundtrip rate problem are the same, when were solving for terms in this problem type, we use the formula Distance 1 = Distance 2 .

In round-trip problems, the time it takes to reach a destination can differ from the time it takes to return because the rate at which the object travels can vary. Furthermore, these problems may only provide the total time to complete the entire trip, from the starting point to the destination and back, rather than separate times for each individual leg of the trip. In these problems, we may need to combine a variable with the total time of the trip, in order to represent the travel times for the different legs.

For example, if a trip to and from a destination takes a total of 10 hours, we can let t hours equal the travel time for one leg of the trip and let (10 t) hours equal the travel time for the other leg of the trip. This should make sense because if the total time is 10 hours and the travel time to the destination is t hours, then the travel time back from the destination must be the difference between the total travel time of the trip (10 hours) and the travel time to the destination (t hours).

Must Know:
Toggle bookmark

Lets now practice everything weve learned about roundtrip rate problems with the following mini-example.

A jet, flying at a constant speed of 675 mph, flew from town X to town Y. Upon reaching town Y, the plane immediately turned and returned to town X on the same route at a constant speed of 450 mph. If the round trip took 5 hours to complete, how many hours did it take the plane to travel from town Y to town X?

We are told the total travel time = 5 hours.

Let t hours = the travel time from town Y to town X, and let (5 t) hours = and the travel time from town X to town Y.

Let's fill in our matrix:

  Rate Time Distance
From X to Y 675 miles hour (5 t) hours  
From Y to X 450 miles hour t hours  

Apply the formula Distance = Rate Time:

  Rate Time Distance
From X to Y 675 miles hour (5 t) hours 675(5 t) miles
From Y to X 450 miles hour t hours 450t miles

For each leg, the same distance is traveled. So, we can write:

Distance From X to Y = Distance From Y to X 675 5t =450t 3,375675t=450t 3,375=1,125t t= 3,375 1,125 =3 hours

It took 3 hours for the jet to fly from town Y to town X.

Text Solution:

This is a round-trip problem. We are told that Jacks rate going to school is 40 mph, and his rate returning home is 60 mph. We are also told that he spent a total of 2 hours traveling to and from school. We can represent the time it took to complete one leg of the trip by t and the time to complete the other leg by (2 t). It does not matter which time we assign to which leg of the trip.

  Rate Time Distance
To School 40 miles hour t hours  
From School 60 miles hour (2 t) hours  

With this information, we can calculate the distances for each leg in terms of t using the distance equation:

distance to school =40 miles hour t hours=40t miles distance from school =60 miles hour 2t hours=12060t miles

  Rate Time Distance
To School 40 miles hour t hours 40t miles
From School 60 miles hour (2 t) hours 120 60t miles

Finally, we can create an equation to determine time. In any round-trip problem, the distance to a destination is always equal to the distance of the return trip. This fact allows us to set up and solve the following equation:

distance to school = distance to home 40t=120-60t t= 120 100 = 6 5 hours

Thus, Jacks time to school is 6 5 hours (remember, we let t equal the time to school). To calculate the distance that Jack drives to school, we can multiply Jacks rate to school (40 mph) by his driving time to school 6 5 hours , which gives us:

distance=ratetime=40 miles hour 6 5 hours=48 miles

However, we need to determine the total distance that Jack travels. Since the distances Jack travels to and from school are equal, the total distance = (48 miles 2) = 96 miles.

Correct answer: C

Laura cycled from city A to city B at a constant speed of 45 kilometers per hour. Upon reaching city B, Laura turned around and cycled the same route back to city A at a constant speed of 30 kilometers per hour. What is the distance from city A to city B?

(1) It took Laura 10 hours to complete the round trip.

(2) It took Laura 6 hours to travel from city B to city A.

Statement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
Statement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
BOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
EACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
Statements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.

Text Solution:

Question Stem Analysis:

Since we are already told the travel speed in each direction, we need some information regarding travel times in order to determine the distance between the two cities.

Statement One Alone:

It took Laura 10 hours to complete the round trip.

If we let t = the travel time from city A to city B, then 10 t = the travel time from city B to city A.

We get the following matrix:

  Rate Time Distance
City A to City B 45 kilometers hour t hours  
City B to City A 30 kilometers hour (10 t) hours  

With this information, we can use the distance formula to express the distance of each leg in terms of t:

distance A to B =rate time distance A to B =45 kilometers hour t hours =45t kilometers

distance B to A =rate time distance B to A =30 kilometers hour 10t hours =30030t kilometers

We get the following matrix:

  Rate Time Distance
City A to City B 45 kilometers hour t hours 45t kilometers
City B to City A 30 kilometers hour (10 t) hours 300 30t kilometers

Since Laura traveled the same distance on each leg of the trip, we can write and solve the following equation:

distance A to B = distance B to A 45t=30030t 75t=300 t= 300 75 =4

So, it took Laura 4 hours to travel from city A to B at a speed of 45 kilometers per hour.

We can use this information to calculate the distance between the two cities:

distance A to B =ratetime distance A to B =45 kilometers hour 4 hours =180 kilometers

Statement one alone is sufficient.

Eliminate answer choices B, C, and E.

Statement Two Alone

It took Laura 6 hours to travel from city B to city A.

We know it took Laura 6 hours to travel from city B to A at a speed of 30 kilometers per hour.

We can use this information to calculate the distance between the two cities:

distance B to A =ratetime distance B to A =30 kilometers hour 6 hours =180 kilometers

Statement two alone is sufficient.

Correct answer: D

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
