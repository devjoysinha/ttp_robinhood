9. Rate Problems

9.20 DS Rate Questions Involving Inequalities

Some DS rate questions will ask us whether some traveler’s rate, time, or distance is greater than or less than some stated rate, time, or distance.

Consider this mini-example.

If it took Quinn 4.5 hours to drive from point A to point B, did Quinn travel less than 270 miles?

Statement one: Quinn’s average speed for the trip was less than 55 miles per hour.

Statement two: Quinn’s average speed for the trip was greater than 45 miles per hour.

Notice that the question—Did Quinn travel less than 270 miles?—asks about Quinn’s travel distance, but the statements provide information about Quinn’s rate.

This suggests that it might be advantageous to rephrase the original question in terms of rate by using the rate × time = distance formula.

If the question in a data sufficiency rate inequality problem involves distance, rate, or time, and the statements both involve a different measurement, then it may be advantageous to rephrase the question in terms of the measurement used in both statements.

In general, if a DS rate question asks us to determine whether some traveler’s rate, time, or distance is greater than or less than some stated rate, time, or distance, then we can solve the problem via the following 6 steps:

Step 1: Write the inequality the question poses.

Step 2: If the measurement asked about in the question is different from the measurements in the statements, then replace the measurement in the question with the formula for that measurement. Otherwise, make no changes to the question.

Step 3: Plug in any given measurements.

Step 4: In the rephrased question, isolate the target measurement.

Step 5: Make any necessary unit conversions.

Step 6: Determine whether the statements are sufficient.

Let’s see how these steps can help us by returning to the mini-example above.

Step 1: Write the inequality the question poses.

⇒ Question: Was Quinn’s travel distance < 270 miles?

Step 2: If the measurement asked about in the question is different from the measurements in the statements, then replace the measurement in the question with the formula for that measurement. Otherwise, make no changes to the question.

Since distance is the measurement asked about in the question, but rate is the measurement in the two statements, we will replace distance with the formula for that measurement, which is rate × time:

⇒WasQuinn's travel distance<270miles?⇒WasQuinn's rate×time<270miles?

Step 3: Plug in any given measurements.

Since we are told Quinn’s driving time was 4.5 hours, we can replace time with 4.5 hours in this inequality:

⇒WasQuinn's rate×time<270miles?⇒WasQuinn's rate×4.5hours<270miles?

Step 4: In the rephrased question, isolate the target measurement.

Since the two statements provide information about Quinn’s rate, it will be useful to take our latest rephrased question and isolate rate.

To do so, we can divide both sides of the inequality by 4.5 hours:

⇒WasQuinn's rate×4.5hours<270miles?⇒WasQuinn's rate<270miles4.5hours?⇒WasQuinn's rate<60mileshour?

Step 5: Make any necessary unit conversions.

Since the rephrased question and the two statements are all in terms of miles per hour, no unit conversions are necessary.

Step 6: Determine whether the statements are sufficient.

Now that everything is in terms of Quinn’s rate, we can more easily analyze the statements.

Statement One: Quinn’s average speed for the trip was less than 55 miles per hour.

If Quinn’s average speed was less than 55 mph, then the answer to the rephrased question is, “YES, Quinn’s rate was definitely less than 60 mph. ”

Thus, statement one is sufficient. Eliminate answer choices B, C, and E.

Statement Two: Quinn’s average speed for the trip was greater than 45 miles per hour.

If Quinn’s average speed for the trip was greater than 45 mph, then we can’t say with certainty whether her rate was less than 60 mph.

For instance, if Quinn’s rate were 50 mph, the answer to the rephrased question would be, “YES, Quinn’s rate was less than 60 mph. ” Conversely, if Quinn’s rate were 70 mph, the answer to the rephrased question would be, “NO, Quinn’s rate was not less than 60 mph. ”

Thus, statement two is not sufficient, we can eliminate answer choice D, and the answer is A.

Text Solution:

Let’s apply the six-step technique to solve the problem:

Step 1: Write the inequality the question poses.

⇒ Question: Was Kate’s travel time < 600 minutes?

Step 2: If the measurement asked about in the question is different from the measurements in the statements, then replace the measurement in the question with the formula for that measurement. Otherwise, make no changes to the question.

Since time is the measurement asked about in the question, but rate is the measurement in the two statements, we will replace time with the formula for time, which is distancerate:

⇒WasKate's travel time<600minutes?⇒WasKate's distanceKate'srate<600minutes?

Step 3: Plug in any given measurements.

Since we are told Kate traveled 120 kilometers, we can add this to the rephrased question:

⇒WasKate's distanceKate'srate<600minutes?⇒Was120 kilometersKate'srate<600minutes?

Step 4: In the rephrased question, isolate the target measurement.

Since we want to rephrase the question in terms of rate, we will isolate rate in the equation:

⇒Was120 kilometersKate'srate<600minutes?⇒Was 120 kilometers<600minutes?×Kate'srate⇒Was120 kilometers600minutes<Kate'srate?⇒Was Kate's rate>120 kilometers600minutes?

Step 5: Make any necessary unit conversions.

The measurement in the statements is miles per hour. So, let’s convert 120 kilometers to miles and convert 600 minutes to hours.

⇒120kilometers×1mile1.6kilometers=1201.6=75miles

⇒600minutes×1hour60minutes=60060=10hours

Now let’s substitute these values into our rephrased question:

⇒Was Kate's rate>120 kilometers600minutes?⇒Was Kate's rate>75 miles10hours?⇒Was Kate's rate>7.5 miles per hour?

So, our rephrased question asks, “Was Kate’s average rate greater than 7.5 miles per hour?”

Step 6: Determine whether the statements are sufficient.

Now that we have rephrased the question to align more closely with the statements, we are ready to analyze the statements.

Statement One Alone:

⇒ Kate’s average rate for the race was less than 9 miles per hour.

This statement does not definitively tell us whether Kate’s average rate was greater than 7.5 miles per hour.

For instance, if her average rate were 8 mph, then the answer to the rephrased question would be, “YES, Kate’s average rate was greater than 7.5 miles per hour. ” Alternatively, if Kate’s average rate were 6 mph, the answer to the rephrased question would be, “NO, Kate’s average rate was not greater than 7.5 miles per hour. ”

Since we can’t answer the rephrased question with certainty, statement one alone is not sufficient.

Eliminate answer choices A and D.

Statement Two Alone:

⇒ Kate’s average rate for the race was more than 8 miles per hour.

If Kate’s average rate was more than 8 mph, then there is only one answer to the rephrased question: “YES, Kate’s average rate was definitely greater than 7.5 miles per hour. ”

Since we can answer the rephrased question with certainty, statement two alone is sufficient. Eliminate answer choices C and E.

Correct answer: B

If it takes a certain fish 30 minutes to swim in a straight line from one end of a pond to the other, did the fish swim more than 1 mile? (1 mile = 1.6 kilometers)

(1) The fish travels at a constant rate that is less than 4 kilometers per hour.

(2) The fish travels at a constant rate that is greater than 3 kilometers per hour.

Statement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
Statement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
BOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
EACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
Statements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.

Text Solution:

Let’s solve this via the six-step technique.

Step 1: Write the inequality the question poses.

⇒ Question: Was the fish’s travel distance > 1 mile?

Step 2: If the measurement asked about in the question is different from the measurements in the statements, then replace the measurement in the question with the formula for that measurement. Otherwise, make no changes to the question.

Since distance is the measurement asked about in the question, but rate is the measurement in the two statements, we will replace distance with rate × time:

⇒Wasdistance>1mile?⇒Wasrate×time>1mile?

Step 3: Plug in any given measurements.

Since the fish swims for 30 minutes, we can replace time with 30 minutes:

⇒Wasrate×time>1mile?⇒Wasrate×(30 minutes)>1mile?

Step 4: In the rephrased question, isolate the target measurement.

Since the two statements provide information about the fish’s rate, we will take our latest rephrased question and isolate rate.

To do so, we can divide both sides of the inequality by 30 minutes to get:

⇒Wasrate×(30 minutes)>1mile?⇒Wasrate>1mile30 minutes?

Step 5: Make any necessary unit conversions.

Since the two statements are in terms of kilometers per hour, we must convert 30 minutes to hours and convert 1 mile to kilometers.

⇒30minutes×1hour60minutes=3060=12=0.5hours

⇒1mile=1.6kilometers1mile=1.6kilometers

We can substitute these values into the question and simplify:

⇒Wasrate>1mile30 minutes?⇒Wasrate>1.6kilometers0.5 hours?⇒Wasrate>3.2kilometers per hour?

So, our rephrased question asks, “Was the fish’s rate greater than 3.2 kilometer per hour?”

Step 6: Determine whether the statements are sufficient.

Statement One Alone:

⇒ The fish travels at a constant rate that is less than 4 kilometers per hour.

This statement does not definitively tell us whether the fish’s rate is greater than 3.2 kilometers per hour.

For instance, the fish’s rate could be 3.9 kilometers per hour, in which case the answer to the rephrased question would be, “YES, the fish’s rate is greater than 3.2 kilometers per hour. ” Alternatively, the fish’s rate could be 3.1 kilometers per hour, in which case the answer to the rephrased question would be, “NO, the fish’s rate is not greater than 3.2 kilometers per hour. ”

Since we can’t answer the rephrased question with certainty, statement one alone is not sufficient.

Eliminate answer choices A and D.

Statement Two Alone:

⇒ The fish travels at a constant rate that is greater than 3 kilometers per hour.

This does not definitively tell us whether the fish’s rate is greater than 3.2 kilometers per hour.

We can again see this by considering the possibilities of 3.9 and 3.1 kilometers per hour for the fish’s rate. One of these rates is below 3.2 kilometers per hour while the other is above it, and they are both greater than 3 kilometers per hour.

Since we can’t answer the rephrased question with certainty, statement two alone is not sufficient.

Eliminate answer choice B.

Statements One and Two Together:

When we combine the statements, we know that the fish’s rate is greater than 3 kilometers per hour but less than 4 kilometers per hour.

This still does not definitively tell us whether the fish’s rate is greater than 3.2 kilometers per hour, as the possible rates we considered earlier (3.1 and 3.9 kilometers per hour) are both between 3 and 4 but yield two different answers to the rephrased question (NO and YES, respectively).

Without more information, both statements together are not sufficient. Eliminate answer choice C.

Correct answer: E

Let’s solve this via the six-step technique.

Step 1: Write the inequality the question poses.

⇒ Question: Was Lin’s average speed > 72 kilometers per hour?

Step 2: If the measurement asked about in the question is different from the measurements in the statements, then replace the measurement in the question with the formula for that measurement. Otherwise, make no changes to the question.

Since rate is the measurement asked about in the question and in the two statements, we will leave the question as it is (for now).

Step 3: Plug in any given measurements.

The stem does not include any additional measurements. So, we will leave our question as is (for now).

Step 4: In the rephrased question, isolate the target measurement.

Since the two statements provide information about the Lin’s rate, we can see that our question—Was Lin’s average speed > 72 kilometers per hour?—already has rate (speed) isolated.

Step 5: Make any necessary unit conversions.

Since the two statements are in terms of meters per second, we must convert 1 hour to seconds and convert 72 kilometers to meters:

⇒72kilometers×1,000meters1kilometer=72,000meters

⇒1hour×3,600seconds1hour=3,600seconds

⇒WasLin's rate>72kilometers per hour?⇒WasLin's rate>72 kilometers1 hour?⇒WasLin's rate>72,000 meters3,600 seconds?⇒WasLin's rate>20 meters per second?

If we again consider 18 and 24 meters per second, we find that both of these rates are greater than 15 meters per second, but one of them is greater than 20 meters per second while the other is less than 20 meters per second.

Since we cannot answer the rephrased question with certainty, statement two alone is not sufficient. Eliminate choice B.

Statements One and Two Together:

When we combine the two statements, we can conclude that Lin’s average speed was greater than 15 meters per second and less than 25 meters per second.

The statements combined do not provide the information required to answer the rephrased question with certainty. Returning to 18 and 24 meters per second, we find that both of these rates are between 15 and 25 meters per second, and (as we have discovered), they give conflicting answers to the rephrased question.

Since we cannot answer the rephrased question with certainty, statements one and two together are not sufficient. Eliminate answer choice C.

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
