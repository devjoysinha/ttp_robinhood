11. Ratios

11.10 Multipart Ratios and the LCM

In some instances, we may need to combine two 2-part ratios to produce a 3-part ratio.

For instance:

Lets say a staff meeting at a hospital has d doctors, n nurses, and a administrators. If d:n = 2:7 and n:a = 7:3, how would we determine the ratio of doctors to administrators in the meeting?

The two ratios have a common variable (n), and n has the same value (7) in both ratios:

Ratio 1: d:n = 2:7

Ratio 2: n:a = 7:3

This means we can combine the two ratios into a single 3-part ratio that compares all three quantities:

Ratio 3: d:n:a = 2:7:3

In his new ratio, d has the value 2, and a has the value 3, so can now see that the ratio of doctors to administrators, d:a, is 2:3.

In the above example, we were helped by the fact that the connecting variable n had the same value in both ratios. That wont always be the case.

For example:

Lets say a box contains w white chips, b black chips, and g green chips. If w:g = 1:4, and g:b = 3:5, then how would we determine the ratio of w to b?

We have two ratios that share a common variable (g), but they do not share the same ratio value of g:

Ratio 1: w:g = 1:4

Ratio 2: g:b = 3:5

In ratio 1, the value of g is 4; in ratio 2, the value of g is 3. Since the ratio values of g are not the same, we cannot immediately combine them.

To combine the ratios, we will create equivalent ratios such that the ratio values of g are the same.

To accomplish this, we can multiply both terms of ratio 1 by 3 while multiplying both terms of ratio 2 by 4 to get the following equivalent ratios:

New Ratio 1: w:g = 1:4= 1(3):4(3) = 3:12

New Ratio 2: g:b = 3:5 = 3(4):5(4) = 12:20

Since the ratios now share the same ratio value of g (12), we can combine them to get the following 3-part ratio:

w:g:b = 3:12:20

At this point, we can see that w:b = 3:20.

Notice that the new shared ratio value of g, 12, is the least common multiple (LCM) of the two original ratio values of g, 4 and 3.

In general, when two ratios share a common variable, we can use the LCM of the values of that common variable to help produce a three-part ratio.

Given two ratios that share one common variable, a three-part ratio can be constructed with the help of the LCM.

Lets practice with a mini-example:

In a certain garden store, the ratio of tomato plants to basil plants is 12 to 7, and the ratio of tomato plants to cucumber plants is 30 to 11. What is the ratio of tomato plants to basil plants to cucumber plants?

Let x = the number of tomato plants, y = the number of basil plants, and z = the number of cucumber plants.

When we translate the given information, we have the following two ratios with x as a common variable:

Ratio 1: tomato plants : basil plants = 12 to 7 x:y = 12:7

Ratio 2: tomato plants : cucumber plants = 30 to 11 x:z = 30:11

In ratio 1, the value of x is 12; in ratio 2, the value of x is 30.

To combine the ratios, we need these x-values to match, and the easiest way to achieve that is to find the LCM of those two values.

The LCM of 12 and 30 is 60. So, lets create equivalent ratios so that the shared ratio value of x is 60. To do so, we will multiply both parts of ratio 1 by 5 and multiply both parts of ratio 2 by 2:

New Ratio 1: x:y = 12:7 = 12(5):7(5) = 60:35

New Ratio 2: x:z = 30:11 = 30(2):11(2) = 60:22

Since these new ratios share a common variable (x) with the same ratio value (60), we can combine the two ratios to get the following 3-part ratio:

x:y:z = 60:35:22

Thus, the ratio of tomato plants to basil plants to cucumber plants is 60:35:22.

In a fishing box with red, blue, and green worms, the ratio of red worms to blue worms is 5 to 6. The ratio of red worms to green worms is 4 to 3. If the total number of worms in the box is a two-digit number, what is the maximum number of blue worms that can be in the box?

Text Solution:

Lets let R = the number of red worms, B = the number of blue worms, and G = the number of green worms.

First, lets translate the given information into equations of ratios. Note that these ratios will have the common variable R:

Ratio 1: R:B = 5:6

Ratio 2: R:G = 4:3

In ratio 1, the value of R is 5, and in ratio 2, the value of R is 4.

The LCM of 4 and 5 is 20. So, lets create equivalent ratios so that the shared ratio value of R is 20. To do so, we will multiply both parts of ratio 1 by 4 and multiply both parts of ratio 2 by 5:

New Ratio 1: R:B = 5:6 = 5(4):6(4) = 20:24

New Ratio 2: R:G = 4:3 = 4(5):3(5) = 20:15

Since both ratios now share the same ratio value of R (20), we can combine the two ratios to get the following 3-part ratio:

R:B:G = 20:24:15.

To determine the number of blue worms, we can introduce a ratio multiplier x, meaning for some value x, R = 20x, B = 24x, and G = 15x.

If x were 1, there would be 20 red worms, 24 blue worms, and 15 green worms, for a total of 59 worms, which is a two-digit number, as required by the question.

If the ratio multiplier were 2, there would be 20(2) = 40 red worms, 24(2) = 48 blue worms, and 15(2) = 30 green worms, for a total of 118 worms, a three-digit number.

Therefore, the ratio multiplier must be 1 in order for the total number of worms to be a two-digit number. This means that 24 is the greatest number of blue worms that can be in the fishing box.

Correct answer: B

Hi Joy, do you need a hint for this example?

At Walton High School, students can be on only one team. Is the number of students who are on the science team at Walton High School greater than 16?

1) At Walton High School, the ratio of the number of students who are on the science team to the number of students who are on the math team is 3 to 4.

2) At Walton High School, the ratio of the number of students who are on the science team to the number of students who are on the chess team is 5 to 2.

Statement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
Statement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
BOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
EACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
Statements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.

PRESENTED BY :
Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor Jeff Miller COO & Expert GMAT Instructor

Text Solution:

Question Stem Analysis:

We must determine whether the number of students who are on the science team at Walton High School is greater than 16.

Let s = the number of students who are on the science team.

Statement One Alone:

At Walton High School, the ratio of the number of students who are on the science team to the number of students who are on the math team is 3 to 4.

Let m = the number of students who are on the math team. This means s:m = 3:4, which only tells us that the number of students on the science team must be a multiple of 3.

So, there could be 3 students or 30 students on the science team. We cant tell. As such, this ratio does not provide enough information to answer the question. Statement one alone is not sufficient.

Eliminate answer choices A and D.

Statement Two Alone:

At Walton High School, the ratio of the number of students who are on the science team to the number of students who are on the chess team is 5 to 2.

Let c = the number of students who are on the chess team. This means that s:c = 5:2, which only tells us that the number of students on the science team must be a multiple of 5.

So, there could be 5 students or 50 students on the science team. We cant tell. As such, this ratio does not provide enough information to answer the question. Statement two alone is not sufficient.

Eliminate answer choice B.

Statements One and Two Together:

The information from both statements provides us with two ratios:

Ratio 1: s:m = 3:4

Ratio 2: s:c = 5:2

Here, the two ratios share s as a common variable. In ratio 1, the value of s is 3, and in ratio 2, the value of s is 5.

The easiest way to make the ratio values of s the same is to find the LCM of those two values.

The LCM of 3 and 5 is 15. So, lets create equivalent ratios so that the shared ratio value of s is 15. To do so, we will multiply both parts of ratio 1 by 5 and multiply both parts of ratio 2 by 3:

New Ratio 1: s:m = 3:4 = 3(5):4(5) = 15:20

New Ratio 2: s:c = 5:2 = 5(3):2(3) = 15:6

Since the ratios now share the same ratio value of s (15), we can combine them to get the following 3-part ratio:

s:c:m =15:6:20

This means that s must be a multiple of 15, so we can now conclude that at least 15 students must be on the science team. Knowing this, however, does not allow us to answer the question since there could be 15, 30, 45, or any other number of students that is a multiple of 15. We cannot whether the number is greater or less than 16.

Correct answer: E

Example 14 DI Data Sufficiency

medium

Need a Little Help?
Hi Joy, do you need a hint for this example?

A group of students consists of Italians, Egyptians, and Ukrainians, and the ratio of Italians to Egyptians is 15 to 8. If there are more than 100 Italians in the group, are there more than 24 Ukrainians?

1) In the group, the ratio of Egyptians to Ukrainians is 12:5.

2) In the group, the total number of Egyptians and Italians is greater than 200.

Statement (1) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (2) alone is not sufficient.
Statement (2) ALONE is sufficient, but statement (1) alone is not sufficient.
BOTH statements TOGETHER are sufficient, but NEITHER statement ALONE is sufficient.
EACH statement ALONE is sufficient.
Statements (1) and (2) TOGETHER are NOT sufficient.
Confirm your answer
Show solution Hide solution
PRESENTED BY :
Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor Jeff Miller COO & Expert GMAT Instructor

Text Solution:

Question Stem Analysis:

Lets let I = the number of Italians, E = the number of Egyptians, and U = the number of Ukrainians.

So, from the given information, we can write:

I:E = 15:8.

I > 100

The question we must answer is Is U > 24?

Statement One Alone:

In the group, the ratio of Egyptians to Ukrainians is 12:5.

From this information, we can write E:U = 12:5.

Together with the ratio provided in the question stem, we now have two ratios to work with:

Ratio 1: I:E = 15:8

Ratio 2: E:U = 12:5

The two ratios share E as a common variable. In ratio 1, the value of E is 8, and in ratio 2, the value of E is 12.

The LCM of 8 and 12 is 24. So, lets create equivalent ratios so that the shared ratio value of E is 24. To do so, we will multiply both parts of ratio one by 3 and multiply both parts of ratio two by 2 to get:

New Ratio 1: I:E = 15:8= 15(3):8(3) = 45:24

New Ratio 2: E:U = 12:5 = 12(2):5(2) = 24:10

Since the ratios now share the same ratio value of E (24), we can combine them to get the following 3-part ratio:

I:E:U =45:24:10

If we let x be a ratio multiplier, this means there will be 45x Italians, 24x Egyptians, and 10x Ukrainians in the group.

Since we are told there are more than 100 Italians in the group, we we see that 45x > 100, and thus the minimum value of x is 3.

If x = 3, then the number of Ukrainians in the group is 10x = 10(3) = 30. So, in this particular case, the answer to the question is, YES, there are more than 24 Ukrainians.

Also, since 3 is the smallest possible value of x, the number of Ukrainians in the group will always be greater than 24.

Thus, statement one alone is sufficient.

Eliminate answer choices B, C and E.

Statement Two Alone:

In the group, the total number of Egyptians and Italians is greater than 200.

From this information, we can write the equation E + I > 200.

Also, from the question stem, we know that I:E = 15:8.

Since we have no information about the value of U, its impossible to determine whether there are more than 24 Ukrainians in the group.

Statement two alone is not sufficient.

Correct answer: A

Hi Joy, do you need a hint for this example?

In a certain kitchen, there are apples, grapes, and plums. The ratio of plums to apples is 5 to 3, while the ratio of apples to grapes is 5 to 6. If there are fewer than 30 grapes, how many plums are in the kitchen?

3

5

10

15

25
Confirm your answer
Show solution Hide solution
PRESENTED BY :
Scott Woodbury-Stewart Founder and Expert GMAT Instructor Jeff Miller COO & Expert GMAT Instructor

Text Solution:

Lets let a = the number of apples, g = the number of grapes, and p = the number of plums.

First, lets translate the given information into equations of ratios:

Ratio 1: p:a = 5:3

Ratio 2: a:g = 5:6

The two ratios share a as a common variable. In ratio one, the value of a is 3, and in ratio two, the value of a is 5.

The LCM of 3 and 5 is 15, so we can combine these ratios if we multiply both parts of ratio one by 5 and multiply both parts of ratio two by 3:

New Ratio 1: p:a = 5:3= 5(5):3(5) = 25:15

New Ratio 2: a:g = 5:6 = 5(3):6(3) = 15:18

Since the ratios now share the same ratio value of a (15), we can combine them to get the following 3-part ratio:

p:a:g = 25:15:18.

If we let x be the ratio multiplier, we can say there are 25x plums, 15x apples, and 18x grapes in the kitchen.

Since we are told there are fewer than 30 grapes, the value of 18x must be less than 30.

If x = 1, then 18x = 18(1) = 18 is indeed less than 30. But if x = 2, then 18(2) = 18(2) = 36 is more than 30.

Thus, since 1 is the only positive integer value of x that will result in fewer than 30 grapes, it must be the case that x = 1, which means the number of plums is 25x = 25(1) = 25.

Correct answer: E

Coordinate Geometry is Currently Tested on the GMAT
Updated on: Jul 2025

While most geometrical concepts were removed from the GMAT Focus Edition, which GMAC now refers to simply as "the GMAT" once again, Coordinate Geometry is still tested.

In addition, some basic geometry concepts, including calculating the area and perimeter of shapes like squares, triangles, and circles, are still tested on the current GMAT. However, whenever a geometry formula outside of coordinate geometry is required, it will be provided in the question stem.
